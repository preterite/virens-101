## Synthesis

Something shifts this week. For the first seven weeks, the emphasis has been on *producing*—generating fragments, accumulating sources, capturing material before you knew what it was for. That work doesn't stop, but the center of gravity moves. You now have enough stuff that managing it becomes its own problem. Welcome to Movement 3: Operating at Scale.

Fred Brooks [[@brooks1986silve]] opens his famous essay "No Silver Bullet" with a monster: "Of all the monsters who fill the nightmares of our folklore, none terrify more than werewolves, because they transform unexpectedly from the familiar into horrors." The werewolf isn't frightening because it's alien—it's frightening because it *used to be someone you knew*. Brooks is writing about software projects that seem manageable until they suddenly aren't, but his image applies to any accumulated work. Your corpus of fragments isn't the same thing it was four weeks ago. It's transformed. It has weight, scope, internal relationships you didn't plan. The familiar pile of your own writing has become something that makes demands on you.

Brooks's central distinction helps name what's happening. He separates *essential* complexity from *accidental* complexity. Essential complexity is inherent to the problem itself—it can't be reduced without changing what you're doing. Accidental complexity is an artifact of your tools and methods—it *can* be reduced, and reducing it makes the essential work more approachable. The title is pessimistic: there's no silver bullet that magically slays complexity. But the distinction itself is useful. When something feels hard, ask: is this difficulty essential (inherent to the intellectual problem) or accidental (a product of how I've set things up)?

Here's an example close to home. Writing a coherent argument from multiple sources is *essentially* complex—it requires holding different perspectives in mind, finding connections, making choices about what to foreground. That complexity doesn't go away with better tools. But *finding* those sources, *organizing* your notes, *formatting* your citations—that's accidental complexity. A good system reduces it. A bad system amplifies it. The work you've done building your Zotero library, your folder structure, your fragment corpus—that's accidental-complexity reduction. You're not smarter than you were in Week 1, but you have infrastructure that makes the essential work more approachable.

This is where the mill/store distinction becomes useful. Charles Babbage—the nineteenth-century mathematician who designed mechanical computers before electronics existed—separated the *store* (where data accumulates) from the *mill* (where operations transform it). Your corpus is the store: fragments, sources, notes, drafts piling up. But accumulation alone doesn't produce anything. The mill is what transforms stored material into something new: selecting, combining, compressing, connecting. Movement 3 is about learning to operate the mill. The six moves you've been practicing—capture, classify, route, distill, connect, publish—shift emphasis. M1 was heavy on capture. M2 added classify and route. Now distill and connect take center stage.

But here's the turn Brooks makes late in his essay, and it matters for what you're doing. He argues that the metaphor of *building* software—assembling components according to a blueprint—has outlived its usefulness. "Let us turn to nature," he writes, "and study complexity in living things, instead of just the dead works of man... The secret is that it is grown, not built." Your corpus isn't a building under construction. It's closer to something that grows—or, in the language we've been using since Jackson, something that transforms from corpse to compost to garden. The fragments you wrote aren't dead lumber waiting to be assembled. They're material undergoing transformation. Some will combine in ways you didn't anticipate. Some will decompose into other things. The werewolf version of this is terrifying: the familiar becomes monstrous. The garden version is generative: the dead becomes alive.

Today's tech session, "What You're Automating," asks a pointed question: when you set up a workflow, what exactly are you delegating to the system, and what judgment are you preserving for yourself? Automation isn't magic—it's a transfer of labor from your future self to your past self (who set up the system) and to the tools themselves. Some transfers are wise: let Zotero handle citation formatting so you can focus on what sources *mean*. Some transfers are risky: if you automate too much classification, you might miss connections that don't fit your categories. Brooks would say: automate the accidental, preserve judgment over the essential.

One more concept from Brooks: *graceful degradation*. Well-designed systems don't collapse entirely when something goes wrong—they fail partially, preserving what they can. Your corpus is designed to degrade gracefully. If one fragment is weak, the whole portfolio doesn't collapse. If you misclassify a source, you can reclassify it later. If a draft doesn't work, the material remains available for recombination. This is different from a house-of-cards model where everything depends on everything else. The fragments you've accumulated aren't load-bearing walls; they're modular components. The system survives partial failure. That's not a flaw—it's a feature of anything complex enough to grow.

By the end of this week, you'll have taken stock of what you actually have (the corpus inventory), drafted BEAM Portfolio entries, gotten evaluative feedback on your source work, and started preparing for next week's conferences. The question isn't whether you have material—you do, probably more than you realize. The question is whether you'll experience its transformation as werewolf or as garden.

### Block Details

#### Block 1: "What You're Automating"

**Type**: T5 (Tech instruction)

**Duration**: 25 minutes

**Materials**: Laptops, student folder systems, Zotero libraries, sample workflow diagrams

**Opening** (3 min): Frame the session: "You've built systems over the past seven weeks—folders, Zotero, naming conventions, fragment routines. Today we ask: what are those systems actually *doing* for you? What labor have you delegated, and what judgment have you preserved?"

**Main Activity** (18 min): 
1. **Audit exercise** (8 min): Students map their current workflow by answering:
   - Where does new material enter your system? (capture points)
   - How do you decide where things go? (classification logic)
   - What happens automatically vs. what requires your decision each time?
   - Where do things get lost or stuck?

2. **Essential vs. accidental discussion** (10 min): In pairs, students identify:
   - One piece of accidental complexity their system has reduced
   - One piece of accidental complexity that still slows them down
   - One essential complexity they can't (and shouldn't) automate away

**Closing** (4 min): Brief share-out. Introduce Brooks's framing: "Automate the accidental, preserve judgment over the essential." Preview the corpus inventory in Block 2.

**Connection**: [[outcome-6-workflow-technologies]]; prepares for corpus inventory; applies Brooks's framework immediately

#### Block 2: Corpus Inventory

**Type**: W

**Duration**: 25 minutes

**Materials**: Student folders (OneDrive), fragment tracking sheet, inventory template

**Opening** (3 min): "Before you can operate at scale, you need to know what you have. This isn't just counting—it's *seeing* your corpus as a system with properties."

**Main Activity** (19 min): Students conduct a structured inventory:
1. **Count**: How many fragments? How many sources in Zotero?
2. **Categorize**: What topics/themes appear most? What's missing?
3. **Assess**: Which fragments feel alive (could develop further)? Which feel dead (might become compost for other work)?
4. **Identify**: Where are the connections you haven't made yet?

Students record findings on inventory template.

**Closing** (3 min): "You probably have more than you thought. The question for the rest of the semester: what will you make of it?" Transition to BEAM Portfolio work.

**Connection**: [[outcome-5-textual-accumulation]]; makes accumulation visible; prepares for BEAM Portfolio framing

#### Block 3: BEAM Portfolio Entry Drafting

**Type**: W

**Duration**: 25 minutes

**Materials**: BEAM Portfolio assignment sheet, Zotero libraries, Prompt 8.3 handout

**Opening** (3 min): Review BEAM categories briefly:
- **B**ackground: What does this source help readers understand?
- **E**xhibit: What evidence does it provide for analysis?
- **A**rgument: What position does it take that you're responding to?
- **M**ethod: What approach does it model that you might use?

Remind students: a single source might serve multiple functions in different contexts.

**Main Activity** (19 min): Students draft BEAM Portfolio entries for 1-2 sources. Each entry should:
- Identify the source and its BEAM function(s)
- Explain *why* this source matters to their developing project
- Include at least one properly integrated quotation (SEAR)

Prompt 8.3 ("Concept Tracing") available as alternative/supplement.

**Closing** (3 min): Checkpoint: "Who has at least two entries ready for peer review?" Address common questions. Remind students that Cycle 4 uses Evaluative (DES) protocol.

**Connection**: [[beam-portfolio-assignment-course-v101]]; applies BEAM and SEAR from Weeks 6-7; prepares for Cycle 4

#### Block 4: Eli Review Cycle 4

**Type**: P

**Materials**: Laptops, Eli Review access, Cycle 4 criteria

**Opening** (3 min): Review the Evaluative (DES) protocol:

## Instructor Notes

### Facilitation Tips

- **Werewolf anxiety**: Some students will recognize the feeling—their corpus *has* become something unfamiliar. Validate this. The synthesis offers two frames (werewolf/garden); help students see both are about transformation, and they have some choice in how they experience it.

- **Essential/accidental application**: Students may struggle to distinguish essential from accidental complexity in their own work. Use concrete examples: "Formatting citations is accidental—Zotero handles it. Deciding *which* sources matter is essential—that's your job."

- **T5 depth**: "What You're Automating" can surface real frustrations with tools. That's useful data. If students identify accidental complexity they haven't solved, help them see options (different tool, different workflow, accepting the friction).

- **Corpus inventory surprise**: Many students will discover they have more material than they realized. This is the point. Help them see the inventory as good news (you have resources) rather than overwhelm (you have too much).

- **Evaluative feedback transition**: Cycle 4 uses DES (evaluative). Some students find this harder to give or receive. Remind them: direct feedback is a gift. Vague praise doesn't help revision.

- **Conference prep**: Block 6 assigns Prompt 9.4 as homework. Check that students understand what to bring: a *revisable* fragment (not their best work, but work with potential) and *real* questions (not performance questions).

### Flexibility Points

- **Block 2 depth**: Corpus inventory can be quick (15 min) or extended (full block) depending on how much students need to process. If they're overwhelmed, extend; if they're ready, move to Block 3.

- **Block 3/Prompt 8.3**: Prompt 8.3 (Concept Tracing) is optional. Students deep in BEAM entries may skip it; students who need a different angle may find it useful.

- **Block 6 balance**: Dev log and conference prep compete for time. If students have been keeping logs consistently, minimize that portion; if logs are neglected, emphasize them.
