## Overview

Your portfolio is assessed holistically using rubrics aligned with all seven course learning outcomes. Rather than grading individual pieces separately, the entire portfolio is evaluated for evidence of outcome achievement.

**Assessment approach**: Criterion-referenced (did you meet outcomes?) not norm-referenced (how do you compare to peers?)

**Portfolio weight**: 50% of course grade

**See also**: [[portfolio-requirements-content-v101|What to include in portfolio]]

**Justification**: [[portfolio-rubric-reasons-v101#Holistic Assessment Philosophy|Why holistic grading?]]

---

## How Portfolios Are Graded

### Assessment Process

**Step 1: Completeness check**
- All required components present? (cover letter, 2-3 polished pieces, process evidence, process archaeology)
- Organized according to specifications?
- Meets minimum length requirements?

**Incomplete portfolios**: Cannot earn above 60% (D-level) regardless of quality

**Step 2: Holistic reading**
- Read cover letter first (student's argument for their grade)
- Read polished pieces with attention to outcome evidence
- Review process evidence for substantive revision
- Consider process archaeology for metacognitive growth

**Step 3: Outcome-by-outcome assessment**
- For each of 7 outcomes, rate portfolio evidence as: Exceptional / Proficient / Developing / Limited
- Look across entire portfolio (not single piece) for outcome evidence
- Consider both products (polished pieces) and processes (revision, reflection)

**Step 4: Overall grade determination**
- Translate outcome ratings into percentage
- Consider holistic impression (portfolio coherence, professional presentation)
- Apply grade distribution philosophy (see below)

## Outcome-Based Assessment

### How Each Outcome Is Assessed

For each outcome, portfolios are evaluated holistically for evidence across all components:

| Outcome | Portfolio Evidence Assessed |
|---------|---------------------------|
| **1. Rhetorical Awareness** | Polished pieces demonstrate audience awareness; cover letter articulates rhetorical choices |
| **2. Critical Reading** | Citations in polished pieces; analysis of course readings; engagement with difficulty in dev logs |
| **3. Composing Processes** | Process evidence shows multiple drafts; dev logs document workflow; system map shows evolution |
| **4. Research & Source Use** | BEAM portfolio work cited; sources integrated rhetorically; Zotero use documented |
| **5. Constraint Awareness** | Spec documents analyze constraints; cover letter discusses genre expectations; convention awareness |
| **6. Collaboration** | Peer feedback received and used; reflective one-pagers cite specific comments; revision responds to feedback |
| **7. Metacognition** | Cover letter argues with evidence; process archaeology shows growth; failure-as-diagnostic evident |

**See detailed rubrics below for each outcome.**

## Outcome 1: Rhetorical Awareness

**Outcome statement**: Compose texts that demonstrate awareness of audience, purpose, genre, and context.

### Rubric

**Exceptional (90-100%)**:
- Polished pieces show sophisticated audience adaptation across different rhetorical situations
- Cover letter explicitly analyzes rhetorical choices made ("I structured the opening to engage readers unfamiliar with...")
- Evidence of strategic genre awareness (choosing spec doc vs. essay for specific purpose)
- Demonstrates understanding that conventions are rhetorical tools, not arbitrary rules

**Proficient (80-89%)**:
- Polished pieces demonstrate clear audience awareness
- Cover letter identifies rhetorical situation for each piece
- Evidence of appropriate genre choices
- Conventions handled appropriately for context

**Developing (70-79%)**:
- Polished pieces show emerging audience awareness but inconsistent application
- Cover letter mentions audience/purpose but doesn't analyze choices
- Some genre awareness but may default to academic essay
- Conventions mostly appropriate with occasional lapses

**Limited (Below 70%)**:
- Polished pieces lack clear audience awareness
- Cover letter doesn't address rhetorical situation
- Little evidence of genre awareness beyond academic essay
- Convention issues interfere with communication

---

## Outcome 2: Critical Reading

**Outcome statement**: Engage with texts through multiple reading practices, including difficulty as a productive site of learning.

### Rubric

**Exceptional (90-100%)**:
- Polished pieces engage substantively with course readings, showing deep understanding
- Cover letter discusses how engaging with difficult readings produced learning
- Dev logs show "failure-as-diagnostic" approach to reading difficulty
- Evidence of multiple reading modes (Salvatori: literal, interpretive, reflective)

**Proficient (80-89%)**:
- Polished pieces cite and engage with course readings appropriately
- Cover letter references readings that influenced thinking
- Dev logs show engagement with reading challenges
- Evidence of moving beyond surface reading

**Developing (70-79%)**:
- Polished pieces cite readings but engagement is surface-level
- Cover letter mentions readings but doesn't show depth
- Dev logs note reading difficulty but don't treat it as productive
- Limited evidence of multiple reading practices

**Limited (Below 70%)**:
- Polished pieces don't meaningfully engage with readings
- Cover letter doesn't reference course readings
- No evidence of treating difficulty as productive
- Reading appears purely instrumental (mining for quotes)

---

## Outcome 3: Composing Processes

**Outcome statement**: Develop and document flexible, recursive writing processes that include invention, drafting, revision, and reflection.

### Rubric

**Exceptional (90-100%)**:
- Process evidence shows substantive revision across multiple cycles (reconceptualization, not just editing)
- Dev logs demonstrate sophisticated process awareness and strategic problem-solving
- System map shows evolution of workflow across semester
- Cover letter analyzes own process development with specific examples

**Proficient (80-89%)**:
- Process evidence shows genuine revision (not just surface editing)
- Dev logs document process consistently
- System map accurately represents workflow
- Cover letter discusses process growth

**Developing (70-79%)**:
- Process evidence shows some revision but mostly surface-level
- Dev logs present but often generic or perfunctory
- System map basic but functional
- Cover letter mentions process but doesn't analyze development

**Limited (Below 70%)**:
- Process evidence minimal or shows only editing, not revision
- Dev logs missing or unhelpful
- System map incomplete or doesn't reflect actual process
- Cover letter doesn't address process

---

## Outcome 4: Research and Source Use

**Outcome statement**: Find, evaluate, and integrate sources using disciplinary conventions and rhetorical awareness.

### Rubric

**Exceptional (90-100%)**:
- Sources integrated rhetorically (not just cited for credibility)
- Evidence of BEAM framework application (Background, Exhibit, Argument, Method)
- Zotero use documented, shows sophisticated organization
- Cover letter discusses source selection and integration strategies

**Proficient (80-89%)**:
- Sources appropriately integrated and cited
- Evidence of BEAM awareness
- Zotero use evident
- Cover letter mentions research process

**Developing (70-79%)**:
- Sources present but integration mechanical
- Limited BEAM awareness (mostly Background sources)
- Minimal Zotero documentation
- Cover letter doesn't discuss research

**Limited (Below 70%)**:
- Few or no sources beyond course readings
- No evidence of BEAM framework
- No Zotero documentation
- Research process invisible

**Note**: Research-heavy portfolios are not required. Students may demonstrate this outcome through engagement with course readings rather than external research.

---

## Outcome 5: Constraint Awareness

**Outcome statement**: Analyze and negotiate the constraints that shape writing situations, including genre conventions, audience expectations, and material affordances.

### Rubric

**Exceptional (90-100%)**:
- Spec documents or analyses explicitly identify and analyze constraints
- Cover letter discusses constraint negotiation across different pieces
- Evidence of treating conventions as negotiable rather than fixed
- Demonstrates understanding that constraints enable as well as limit

**Proficient (80-89%)**:
- Clear awareness of genre constraints
- Cover letter identifies how constraints shaped choices
- Evidence of working within constraints appropriately
- Understanding that conventions serve rhetorical purposes

**Developing (70-79%)**:
- Emerging constraint awareness
- Cover letter mentions constraints but doesn't analyze
- Follows conventions but may not understand why
- Limited discussion of how constraints shaped work

**Limited (Below 70%)**:
- Little evidence of constraint awareness
- Cover letter doesn't address constraints
- Conventions handled as rules rather than rhetorical choices
- No analysis of how constraints affect writing

---

## Outcome 6: Collaboration and Feedback

**Outcome statement**: Give and receive feedback generatively, using peer response to strengthen writing and develop critical reading skills.

### Rubric

**Exceptional (90-100%)**:
- Process evidence shows substantive engagement with peer feedback
- Reflective one-pagers cite specific peer comments and explain revision choices
- Evidence of using feedback selectively and strategically (not just accepting all suggestions)
- Cover letter analyzes how collaboration shaped thinking

**Proficient (80-89%)**:
- Process evidence shows genuine engagement with peer feedback
- Reflective one-pagers reference specific feedback received
- Evidence of responsive revision
- Cover letter mentions peer feedback role

**Developing (70-79%)**:
- Some evidence of considering peer feedback
- Reflective one-pagers mention feedback generically
- Limited evidence that feedback shaped revision
- Cover letter briefly notes peer review

**Limited (Below 70%)**:
- Little evidence of engaging with peer feedback
- Reflective one-pagers don't cite specific feedback
- No visible connection between feedback and revision
- Cover letter doesn't address collaboration

---

## Outcome 7: Metacognitive Awareness

**Outcome statement**: Reflect on and articulate one's own writing processes, challenges, and development to enable transfer to new contexts.

### Rubric

**Exceptional (90-100%)**:
- Cover letter argues convincingly with specific portfolio evidence
- Process archaeology shows sophisticated self-awareness and growth over time
- Evidence of treating difficulty as diagnostic rather than failure
- Transfer language evident ("This strategy I developed could apply to...")

**Proficient (80-89%)**:
- Cover letter makes clear claims supported by portfolio evidence
- Process archaeology shows development
- Evidence of process reflection
- Some transfer awareness

**Developing (70-79%)**:
- Cover letter present but claims not well-supported
- Process archaeology shows basic reflection
- Limited evidence of learning from difficulty
- Minimal transfer language

**Limited (Below 70%)**:
- Cover letter generic or unsupported
- Process archaeology perfunctory
- No evidence of metacognitive growth
- No transfer awareness

## Overall Grade Determination

### Translating Outcome Ratings to Grades

**A-range (90-100%)**:
- Exceptional rating in 4-5 outcomes
- Proficient or better in all 7 outcomes
- Holistically demonstrates sophisticated achievement

**B-range (80-89%)**:
- Proficient rating in 5-7 outcomes
- May have 1-2 Developing ratings
- Holistically demonstrates solid achievement

**C-range (70-79%)**:
- Developing rating in 4-5 outcomes
- May have Limited ratings in 1-2 outcomes
- Holistically demonstrates inconsistent achievement

**D-range (60-69%)**:
- Limited ratings in 3+ outcomes
- Incomplete portfolio
- Holistically demonstrates minimal achievement

**F (Below 60%)**:
- Limited ratings in most outcomes
- Significantly incomplete portfolio
- Minimal evidence of engagement

### Holistic Adjustment

Final grade may be adjusted Â±5 percentage points based on:
- **Professional presentation**: Organization, file naming, readability
- **Portfolio coherence**: How well components work together
- **Evidence quality**: Strength of argument in cover letter
- **Growth trajectory**: Visible development across semester

## Common Questions

### Do all polished pieces need to demonstrate all outcomes?

**No.** Outcomes are assessed **across the entire portfolio**, not piece by piece.

Example: One polished piece might strongly demonstrate Outcomes 1, 2, 5 while another demonstrates 3, 6, 7. Together they cover all outcomes.

### What if I excel at some outcomes but struggle with others?

**Your overall grade reflects the whole portfolio.** Strong achievement in some outcomes can offset developing work in others, but all outcomes should show at least Developing-level evidence.

### How much does the cover letter matter?

**Significantly.** The cover letter is your opportunity to **argue for your grade** by directing attention to portfolio evidence. A weak cover letter makes it harder to see your achievement.

### Can I revise my portfolio after submission?

**No.** Portfolio submissions are final. However, you can schedule a **portfolio preview conference** in Week 12 to get feedback before finalizing.

### What if my portfolio is incomplete?

Incomplete portfolios (missing required components) cannot earn above 60% regardless of quality. Ensure all components are present.

## Using This Rubric

### During Portfolio Assembly (Weeks 13-15)

Use this rubric to:
- **Self-assess**: Which outcomes do I have strong evidence for? Which need more work?
- **Select pieces**: Choose polished pieces that collectively demonstrate all outcomes
- **Draft cover letter**: Make sure to address each outcome with portfolio evidence
- **Check gaps**: If you can't find evidence for an outcome, revise a piece to demonstrate it

### In Your Cover Letter

Reference this rubric explicitly:
- "For Outcome 3 (Composing Processes), my portfolio demonstrates Proficient-level achievement through..."
- "The evidence for this rating includes [specific drafts, dev log entries, system map annotations]..."

This shows you understand the assessment criteria and can argue strategically.
