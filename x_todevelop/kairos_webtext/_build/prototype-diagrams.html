Two overlaid structures: the W-P-R rhythm mapped onto production-distribution-consumption-reproduction, with value appropriation annotated at each stage. The inner cycle shows the process; the outer annotations show who captures value and how.

The outer columns reveal what standard composition pedagogy leaves implicit: value doesn't just circulate — it gets appropriated at every stage, by multiple actors, for different purposes. When the institution captures assessment data at the consumption stage, that's an extraction the student may not recognize. When the field captures method at reproduction, that's the economy of scholarship operating through the individual classroom. Making these appropriations visible is the economic argument applied to composition.

Diagram 2: Corpse → Compost → Garden

The three-state transformation arc. The leftward state looks complete but is inert; the middle state looks chaotic but is productive; the rightward state is composed from decomposition, not despite it.

Figure: Textual transformation across the course arc

The bottom loop — capital enables future labor — is the transfer claim. What students build in one course becomes infrastructure for the next. The rubric that assesses only the garden (M4 portfolio) misses the point: the corpse and compost stages are where the real transformation happens. Assessment that extracts value only at harvest time is extractive agriculture.
