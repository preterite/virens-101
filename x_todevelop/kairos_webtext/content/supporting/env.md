Composition's disciplinary history gives us reason to be suspicious of decomposing writing into discrete operations. The trajectory is well documented, if not always recognized as a single through-line: from Adam Smith's analysis of the division of mental labor, through Charles Babbage's extraction of skill into procedure in _On the Economy of Machinery and Manufactures_ (1832), to Catharine Beecher's rationalization of educational and domestic labor, to Alexander Bain's codification of the modes of discourse that would organize composition instruction for a century. At each stage, complex literate practice was broken into simpler, more manageable, more _countable_ units. At each stage, the economic logic driving that decomposition became less visible.

Babbage made the economic argument explicitly: any labor process containing operations of varying difficulty can be reorganized so that each operation is performed by a worker whose skill (and therefore wage) matches only that operation's demands. The surplus goes to the organizer. Beecher translated this industrial rationality into the domestic sphere and the schoolroom without announcing the translation. Bain's modes of discourse — description, narration, exposition, argumentation — carried the economic logic further still, organizing writing instruction into units that could be scheduled, sequenced, assessed, and staffed by workers of varying skill levels. By the time the modes reached the twentieth-century textbook, their origin in political economy had been thoroughly naturalized. They appeared as features of discourse itself rather than as managerial technologies imposed upon it.

This webtext presents a pedagogy that decomposes the moves associated with writing as information transfer into discrete operations. We name six — capture, classify, route, distill, connect, publish — and treat them as visible, countable, and therefore designable. We track textual accumulation as capital. We sequence constraints strategically across a semester. Readers familiar with the history above may ask: _how is this not the same thing as conventional process pedagogy?_

The answer requires three distinctions.

### cat {title="what histories have obscured"}

The historical trajectory's characteristic move is submersion. In composition's history, economic operations enter the classroom without announcing it. Bain's modes did not present themselves as technologies for organizing instructional labor; they presented themselves as natural features of written language. The credit hour did not announce itself as a mechanism for commensurating heterogeneous temporal experiences; it presented itself as a neutral measure.

VIRENS 101 reverses this. The course names its operations as economic in character — writing _as_ labor, fragments _as_ capital, accumulation _as_ value formation. Students encounter this vocabulary not as a description of what writing naturally is but as a framework imposed for specific purposes, available for interrogation. The natualization of Bain's modes forecloses possibilities. A student who can see that "capture" is an operation being asked of her can ask _why this operation, why now, who benefits_.

### pipe {title="who benefits from decomposition"}

Who profits from decomposition determines its character. The Smith-Babbage-Beecher-Bain trajectory served institutional efficiency: instructor replaceability, throughput maximization, administrative legibility. The decomposed operations made it easier to staff, schedule, and assess writing instruction — not easier to write. (insert footnote about Fred Kemp's appropriative cycle of TA deskilling)

The operations named in VIRENS 101 serve the practitioner. When a student learns to distinguish capturing from classifying, she gains leverage over a process previously experienced as undifferentiated effort. The accumulated fragments are _her_ capital, compounding across a semester into a corpus she can draw on, reorganize, and extend. The institution cannot efficiently assess whether a student's routing decisions were sound, or whether her distillation preserved what mattered — these judgments require the kind of situated expertise that resists bureaucratic capture. The decomposition serves the writer's agency, not the administrator's spreadsheet.

### endif {title="where decomposition must stop"}

Babbage's most consequential insight was that the line between "requires skill" and "can be delegated to a cheaper worker" is movable. Any operation that seems irreducibly complex can, with sufficient analysis, be broken down further. His intellectual descendants in composition — the paragraph model, the five-paragraph essay, the rubric — pushed decomposition without a principled stopping point.

VIRENS 101 identifies one. The course distinguishes between operations that can be decomposed and delegated — routing, classification, formatting, templating — and labor whose value is constituted in the temporal encounter itself. Reading a difficult text across time. Writing through confusion to partial clarity. Sitting with a peer's draft long enough to see what it is trying to become. These are experience goods: their value is destroyed by externalization, by any attempt to extract the encounter from the person having it. You can decompose the _preparation_ for such encounters — that is what the six operations largely do — but you cannot decompose the encounter without destroying what makes it valuable.

This is not an arbitrary limit. It is a structural feature of a category of goods that economic theory recognizes and that composition's prior decomposition programs lacked. Bain's modes had no such brake. The VIRENS vocabulary does.

### trap {title="why this can go wrong"}

Does making decomposition visible and student-serving fully escape the history it learns from? We are not confident that it does. The same operations that give students leverage could be appropriated for institutional assessment. A six-moves vocabulary is only a slight rearrangement away from a six-point rubric. Fragment counts could become compliance metrics. Accumulation tracking could become surveillance.

What prevents this is not the design alone but the ongoing practice of denaturalization — the insistence that these operations are _imposed_ technologies, not natural features of writing, and that their beneficiary must be continuously interrogated. Grant Miles, the student whose experience threads through this webtext, voices this concern. We do not resolve it. Denaturalization is a practice.

What follows is the environment these variables produce, structured in three layers that do not collapse into one.

The **Vera track** and the **Grant track** narrate experience. Vera designs within this framework; Grant encounters it. Their scenes do not argue — they show what it feels like to plan a constraint sequence and what it feels like to live through one. The order in which their scenes unfold is a doing-order: the sequence of a semester as experienced by a designer and a student.

The **Nexus pages**, which must first be approached through the Grant or Vera tracks, develop the binding theory and method. Each nexus page advances a claim about composition, technology, or value. Together, the twelve nexus pages compose the QUANTITATION sequence — the understanding-order in which the webtext's argument becomes legible to a reader. This sequence is deliberately different from the course's doing-order. The divergence is not incidental: it is itself evidence for the article's central claim that decomposition permits resequencing, and that the order of experience need not match the order of comprehension.

The **course design** — the VIRENS 101 project, its student-facing materials, its assessment architecture, its weekly schedules and constraint sequences — is the evidence. It is the object the nexus pages theorize and the environment the scenes narrate. The network diagram is the exploded view: the entire course, with student-facing materials and teacher-facing apparatus, available for inspection as the empirical basis of the argument.

Experience. Claims. Evidence. Three layers, one webtext.
