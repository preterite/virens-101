# Ulmus (Elm)

## Core Claim

Naming the operations writers perform on information — not merely the temporal stages writers move through — makes those operations distinguishable, specifiable, and available for deliberate sequencing. Traditional process pedagogy names stages: prewriting, drafting, revising, editing, publishing. These terms describe _when_ writers work but leave unnamed _what_ writers do with information at each stage. An operational vocabulary names the work itself: notice (capture), sort (classify), direct (route), compress (distill), link (connect), release into circulation (publish). The two vocabularies are complementary, not competing: process describes the writer's temporal arc, while six moves describes the transformations information undergoes as it passes through that arc. But the two vocabularies that appear to run in parallel are not symmetrical. Process stages are temporal and sequential even when recursive; operations are functional and concurrent. A writer revising is simultaneously reclassifying, rerouting, and reconnecting — three distinct operations collapsed into one stage name. Once operations are named as operations, they can carry further attributes — proportion, relation, dependency — that temporal labels cannot support. This is what makes the integration of systems thinking into composition pedagogy possible: not as imported framework but as the natural consequence of naming operations precisely enough to track how text and attention interact.

## Theoretical Framework

### The Naming Thesis

The intellectual contribution of this nexus is specific: naming operations rather than stages changes what becomes available for analysis and design. The claim draws on Christopher Alexander's concept of pattern languages — named, repeatable solutions to recurring design problems. A pattern language works because naming a solution makes it portable: once "light on two sides of every room" is named as a pattern, designers can recognize it, specify it, combine it with other patterns, and evaluate whether a given design satisfies it. Without the name, the phenomenon exists but cannot be deliberately composed with other phenomena. Naming is not labeling; it is the precondition for composability.

The six moves function as a pattern language for information work. Each move names a transformation that information undergoes: capture brings material from experience into inscription; classify assigns it a type and therefore a relationship to other material; route directs it toward a context where it will be acted upon; distill compresses it toward its essential contribution; connect establishes its relationships to other material; publish releases it into circulation beyond the writer's immediate control. These operations overlap in practice — a writer capturing a quotation is simultaneously classifying it as the kind of thing worth preserving — but remain distinct as processes, just as Alexander's patterns overlap in built space while remaining distinct as design solutions.

The distinction between agent-centered and material-centered vocabulary is where the two frameworks diverge most sharply, and where the appearance of balance between them breaks down. Process pedagogy asks: what is the writer doing? (Inventing. Drafting. Revising.) The six moves ask: what is happening to the information? (Being captured. Being classified. Being routed.) The agent of the first vocabulary is the writer; the agent of the second is the material itself as it moves through transformation. This is not a trivial reframing. When the agent is the writer, the grammar is active and the orientation is toward completing compositions. When the agent is the material, the grammar describes transformation and the orientation shifts toward building infrastructure that accumulates value over time. The writer's labor and the material's trajectory are simultaneous descriptions of the same activity, but they make different things visible.

Charles Babbage's distinction between mill and store — drawn from his _On the Economy of Machinery and Manufactures_ (1832), not the computing works for which he is better remembered — provides the spatial logic for understanding how these vocabularies coexist. The mill is where transformation occurs — efficient operations on material. The six moves are mill operations: capturing, classifying, routing, distilling, connecting, publishing. The store is where abundance accumulates — the corpus, the archive, the portfolio. Process pedagogy describes a writer working between mill and store: pulling from accumulated capital, laboring on it, depositing new material for future use. The critical architectural insight is that the mill is always _within_ the store, not separate from it. Every writing session draws from what has been accumulated and deposits something new. The economic vocabulary from [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_quercus|Quercus]] activates here: operations become countable once named, and countability is what makes the economic frame actionable rather than merely metaphorical. How many captures this week? When did classification begin? What proportion of the corpus has been distilled? These questions become askable only when operations have names distinct from the stages in which they occur. This is the specific node where quantitation — the rhetorical operation of making transformation countable and therefore visible — enters the argument as practice rather than aspiration.

### Process Pedagogy as Inheritance

The six moves vocabulary inherits from process pedagogy; it does not displace it. What the process tradition accomplished is foundational: Donald Murray's insistence that writing is a process not a product; Peter Elbow's demonstration that separating generating from evaluating transforms both; Linda Flower and John Hayes's cognitive process model showing that planning, translating, and reviewing interact recursively rather than proceeding in lockstep; Nancy Sommers's research demonstrating that experienced and novice writers revise differently in kind, not merely degree. These are permanent contributions. The six moves do not contest them.

What the six moves make visible is what the process vocabulary, precisely because of its temporal orientation, sometimes obscures. Sommers's finding about revision is the clearest illustration. Novice writers treat revision as editing — changing words, fixing errors, adjusting surface features. Experienced writers treat revision as reconception — rethinking the relationships among ideas, restructuring arguments, reimagining the text's purpose. Both activities happen during "the revising stage," and process vocabulary names them with the same word. But reconception and surface correction are different operations: reconception is primarily _connecting_ and _reclassifying_ (seeing the material as a different kind of thing, establishing new relationships among its parts), while surface correction is primarily _distilling_ (compressing toward clarity at the sentence level). The process vocabulary cannot help students see this difference because it has only one name for the stage in which both occur. The operational vocabulary can, because it names the work itself rather than the time at which the work happens.

This is extension, not critique. The temporal vocabulary remains necessary — writers do move through stages, recursion is real, and the separation of generating from evaluating is a genuine pedagogical discovery. But the temporal vocabulary is insufficient for the design work that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_alnus|Alnus]] describes: if a course designer wants to sequence constraints deliberately, the designer needs to know not just _when_ students are in the revising stage but _which operations_ the revising stage currently demands, so that the constraints governing those operations can arrive at the right time.

### Scale-Shift and Transferability

A core pedagogical move reveals the six moves as more than course procedures: the scale-shift. What you did with index cards on a classroom table — capturing claims, classifying them by theme, routing them toward an argument, distilling redundancies, connecting related ideas, deciding what to publish — is the same thing a game studio does when managing development assets, the same thing a newsroom does when assembling coverage, the same thing a researcher does when building a dissertation across years of fieldwork. The six moves are not specific to first-year writing. They describe the operations that constitute knowledge work at any scale.

Jenny Edbauer's ecological model of rhetoric, adapted here to economic framing, provides theoretical grounding for the scale-shift. Edbauer argues that rhetorical situations are not discrete containers but circulating ecologies in which texts, affects, and practices move through overlapping networks. The six moves describe precisely these movements: capture enters material into a local ecology; classify positions it within that ecology's categories; route, connect, and distill transform it; publish releases it into wider circulation. What changes across scale is not the operations but the infrastructure that supports them: index cards for a first-year student, a version-controlled repository for a software team, a citation management system for a scholarly project. The operations transfer because they name the work, not the tools.

Doug Downs and Elizabeth Wardle's Writing About Writing (WAW) approach provides the disciplinary context for this transferability claim. WAW argues that writing courses should teach students about writing as a subject of study, giving them a metalanguage for analyzing writing situations they will encounter beyond the course. The six moves function as precisely this kind of metalanguage — not a set of rules for producing acceptable academic prose but a vocabulary for describing what information does as writers work on it, applicable in any context where information moves through human attention. The difference from WAW's typical implementation is the economic grounding: the six moves are not only analytical categories but operational ones, describing the labor that transforms information from one state to another. A student who can name these operations can also ask who benefits from each transformation and at what cost — the questions that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_olea|Olea]] pursues.

### Relationship to Alnus's Process Scholars

Several scholars central to this page's argument about process pedagogy — Perl, Elbow, Flower — also appear in [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_alnus|Alnus]]'s analysis of how composition studies independently discovered cognitive load theory's core findings. The overlap is real but the argumentative function differs. Alnus treats these scholars as evidence for _convergent discovery_: each arrived, through phenomenological or practitioner methods, at findings that CLT confirmed experimentally. Ulmus treats the same scholars as evidence for a _vocabulary gap_: the temporal language they developed, while revolutionary in what it made visible about writing as process, leaves unnamed the operational dimension that would make their insights available for the kind of deliberate design work constraint sequencing requires. Same figures, different contributions to the argument: convergent discovery in one case, incomplete vocabulary in the other.

## Course Implementation

VIRENS 101 introduces the six moves vocabulary progressively across the semester rather than presenting it as a framework in week one. The progressive introduction is not incidental but deliberate — it enacts the constraint-sequencing logic that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_alnus|Alnus]] theorizes and operationalizes the experience-good principle that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_taxus|Taxus]] establishes. Premature naming would reduce the six moves to a checklist — another set of terms to memorize rather than operations to recognize from the inside. The vocabulary arrives only after students have already performed the operation it names, so that naming becomes recognition rather than imposition. In Sweller's terms, the vocabulary itself is a constraint whose premature arrival would generate extraneous cognitive load: students would be tracking terminology while trying to learn the operations the terminology describes, competing demands on working memory that serve neither goal well.

The introduction follows the semester's four-movement structure. In Movement 1 (Surfacing, Weeks 1–3), students capture fragments — the first operation — without the term "capture" carrying any special technical weight. They are simply writing, regularly, in short units. Capture is operational from the first day and named almost immediately (Weeks 1–2) because the gap between doing and naming is minimal: students can see that what they are doing is bringing material from experience into written form.

Movement 2 (Naming, Weeks 4–7) is where the six moves vocabulary becomes explicit. Classify arrives with BEAM (Week 6) — the operation of sorting sources by their function in an argument. Route has been operational since peer feedback began (Week 3) but is named in Week 7, when students can recognize that the act of directing material toward a particular reader or context is itself a consequential transformation, not merely a logistical step. The deliberate gap between operating and naming — particularly visible in route's four-week lag — is pedagogically productive: students encounter the operation as practice before they have language for it, and the naming moment becomes a recognition event.

Movement 3 (Operating at Scale, Weeks 8–11) introduces the mill/store metaphor as students transfer analog systems to digital. Distill arrives operationally at Week 8 when students perform corpus inventory — the first moment they must decide what their accumulated material _means_ at a level more compressed than any individual fragment. Connect follows as students prepare for conference (Week 9) and discover that their fragments relate to each other in ways they had not planned. The economic framing — labor, capital, value accumulation — becomes available in this movement because students now have enough material to see that past writing is not disposable but productive, still generating value as input to new compositions.

Movement 4 (Synthesis, Weeks 12–15) makes both vocabularies available for portfolio reflection. Publish arrives last (Week 13) because it names the operation that requires all prior operations to have done their work: you can only release into circulation material that has been captured, classified, routed, distilled, and connected. Students in their portfolio reflections can analyze their own work using either vocabulary or both — describing their process temporally (I revised extensively in Week 11) or operationally (I reclassified three fragments and connected them to a source I had not previously routed into the project). The dual vocabulary does not double the analytical burden; it doubles the resolution.
