# Acer (Maple)

## Core Claim

The argument about economy and tecjhnology rests on a syllogism. Technologies substitute capital-intensive processes for labor-intensive processes — this is what technologies do, definitionally, not merely as a tendency of industrial production. Writing is a technology in precisely this sense: orthographic systems, genres, citation conventions, even the sentence as a unit of composition all represent accumulated solutions that reduce the cognitive labor of future writers. If writing is a technology, and technologies perform a transformation that is by definition economic, then composition studies is a necessary site and subject of economic inquiry. The inquiry is not borrowed from another discipline but investigates dynamics already intrinsic to its object.

The move is ontological, not analytical. The composition classroom is an economic site, not merely a site affected by economic forces. A student drafting a paragraph is engaged in production. Peer response is a circulation event. Revision is re-production. These are not analogies for economic activity; they _are_ economic activity — non-market, non-capitalist, but economic in the sense that J. K. Gibson-Graham's diverse-economies framework insists upon: production is production regardless of whether it occurs within market relations. The political economy of composition tradition has documented what happens _to_ writing instruction because of economic pressures; this argument examines what happens _as_ writing instruction because it is already economic.

## Theoretical Framework

### The Genealogy: Marx → Olson → Edwards

The definitional chain has three links, and each adds specificity as the claim travels from political economy through critical pedagogy to composition.

Karl Marx's analysis of machinery and large-scale industry (_Capital_, Volume 1, Part 4) identifies a historical tendency: mechanization substitutes constant capital — machinery, raw materials, accumulated past labor crystallized in equipment — for variable capital, the living labor of workers. This is an observation about industrial production and its dynamics, not a definition of technology per se. Marx describes what happens when capitalist production develops; he does not claim that all technologies, in all contexts, perform this substitution by their nature.

C. Paul Olson, writing about computing technology in education, renders the relationship definitional. In "Who Computes?" (1987), Olson argues that technologies _function to_ replace labor-intensive processes with capital-intensive processes [page TBD]. The formulation is precise and the shift from Marx is consequential. The "-processes" construction moves the claim from a description of industrial machinery to a characterization of what any technology does, including educational and communicative technologies. The mediating source matters: Olson is already writing about pedagogy and technology in schools, making the extension to writing a shorter and better-grounded step than going directly from Marx's factory floor. The term _Marxian_ — describing the study and analytical application of Marx's economic principles, as distinct from _Marxist_, which describes social and political movements aligned with Marx's revolutionary program — applies to the analytical inheritance Olson draws upon and this webtext continues.

Edwards (2014) extends the definition to writing itself and draws the syllogistic conclusion: "If composition scholars understand writing as a technology, and understand as well that technologies function to replace labor-intensive processes with capital-intensive processes and that such a transformation is by definition economic, then all of the field of composition must be taken as necessary site and subject of economic inquiry" (258). The present webtext extends the argument Edwards (2014) established theoretically by enacting it through course design. What that earlier article argued should be done — economic inquiry into composition grounded in the scholarship of economics — this webtext attempts to do, tracing twelve principles through which the labor-capital dynamics of writing instruction become visible, countable, and available for deliberate design.

The genealogy itself does argumentative work. Capital-for-labor substitution accumulates specificity as it moves from political economy through critical pedagogy to composition studies, each link grounding the claim more precisely in the domain where it will be applied. That travel narrative — a concept gaining traction as it passes through disciplines — is itself an instance of the circulation dynamics that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_illicium|Illicium]] traces.

### What Writing Substitutes

Edwards (2014) treats "writing" as a monolithic technology. The webtext's contribution at this nexus is to specify _which features_ of writing perform the capital-for-labor substitution — and this specification is what activates the rest of the QUANTITATION argument.

Orthographic systems substitute visual notation for the labor of memory. Genres substitute conventional structures for the labor of inventing form from scratch each time a writer faces a communicative situation. Citation conventions substitute standardized attribution for the labor of establishing provenance ad hoc. Each of these is a technology in the definitional sense: an accumulated solution, developed and refined over time, that converts what was once labor-intensive into something capital-intensive — available, portable, reusable. Each also has a history of development, adoption, and contestation, which means each embodies accumulated decisions about whose labor counts and whose solutions get preserved. A technology calibrated for one range of contexts performs differently when applied to another, and the word for what a technology does often arrives only after the hands have already known its use.

This specification connects forward to [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_ulmus|Ulmus]]: each named operation in the six moves is itself a technological substitution. Capture substitutes inscription for unaided recall. Classify substitutes category systems for case-by-case evaluation. Route substitutes organizational infrastructure for ad hoc discovery. The six moves are not only operations but _technologies_ — capital-intensive solutions that accumulated over time and now reduce the labor cost of information work. Once named as technologies (Ulmus) and counted as operations ([[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_quercus|Quercus]]), they become available for the kind of economic analysis this nexus argues composition requires.

Walter Ong provides the phenomenological grounding: writing restructures consciousness, which means writing is not merely a tool applied to thought but a technology that transforms the thinker. This is the experiential side of the substitution — capital replaces labor, but the replacement changes what labor _is_ and what the laborer _can do_. The substitution is not neutral exchange; it is transformation.

Charles Bernstein, in "Blood on the Cutting Room Floor," makes the political-economic argument that Ong's phenomenological account requires but does not itself provide. Writing technologies distribute control unevenly: the choice of which technology to adopt is simultaneously a choice about who controls the means of textual production. Bernstein's attention to how the word processor promises access while potentially domesticating the writer — replacing the exploratory labor of composition with the efficiency of formatting — identifies a risk inherent in every capital-for-labor substitution. The substitution is real, but its consequences depend on who designs the technology, who deploys it, and who captures the value it produces. This is the distribution question that follows from the definition: once you see writing as economic, you must ask _whose_ economy.

### The Ontological Move

The distinction matters because it determines whether economic vocabulary is imported or discovered. If the classroom is merely _affected by_ economic forces — adjunctification, austerity, student debt, assessment regimes — then economic analysis is a lens applied from outside, useful but detachable. If the classroom _is_ an economic site, then economic analysis investigates what is already happening there, and the vocabulary is not imported but uncovered. The political economy of composition tradition, as represented in works like Tony Scott's _Dangerous Writing_ and the 2012 _JAC_ special issue on economies of writing, has powerfully documented the external forces. This argument addresses the internal dynamics: the economic activity constituted by the acts of writing, response, and revision themselves.

Gibson-Graham's diverse-economies framework is essential here. Pierre Bourdieu's "capital" — cultural capital, social capital, symbolic capital — operates by analogy: these are _like_ economic capital in that they can be accumulated, exchanged, and converted. The analogy is productive but remains metaphorical; the domains are structurally parallel but ontologically separate. Gibson-Graham refuse the metaphor. In their framework, non-market production is not analogous to production; it _is_ production, taking diverse forms (self-directed, gift, communal, institutional, cooperative) that operate alongside and within and against market production. When a student drafts a paragraph, the labor is real, the product is real, the value is real — regardless of whether the product enters a market. This is why the webtext's economic vocabulary (labor, capital, appropriation, circulation, fixed and liquid capital) draws on the heterodox tradition Edwards (2014) identifies as missing from composition studies' engagement with economic questions: Gibson-Graham, Resnick and Wolff, Horner's materialist critique. The vocabulary is not figurative. It describes what it names.

### Machine Delegation as Test Case

The definitional claim finds its contemporary test case in AI writing tools. Large language models are capital-intensive processes substituting for labor-intensive processes — this is precisely what they are, definitionally, not by analogy or provocation. The training data is accumulated past labor crystallized in parameters; the output substitutes computational capital for the labor of composition. The Acer definition does not need to be stretched or adapted to accommodate AI; AI is simply the latest and most dramatic instance of the substitution the definition describes.

What the definition _does_ is reframe the question. The interesting question about AI in composition is not "is it cheating?" or "should we allow it?" but rather: _which_ labor does the substitution replace, and who captures the resulting value? If the substituted labor is the formatting and surface-correction work that Bernstein worries the word processor already domesticates, then the substitution may free students for higher-order composing. If the substituted labor is the generative, recursive, experience-dependent work that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_taxus|Taxus]] identifies as an experience good, then the substitution destroys the value it claims to produce. The Acer definition says all technologies substitute capital for labor; Taxus identifies the limit case where that substitution is structurally counterproductive — where the labor _is_ the value, and replacing it with capital produces nothing worth having.

## Course Implementation

VIRENS 101 splits the technology-as-substitution argument across the semester, and the split is deliberate rather than accidental. Ong at Week 2 addresses writing-as-technology phenomenologically: writing restructures consciousness, which means writing _does something to you_ that can only be encountered through doing. This is the experience-good framing — the substitution's consequences require experience to understand. Brooks at Week 8 ("What You're Automating") addresses the substitution logic directly: technology replaces labor with capital, and the design question is whether a given substitution serves or undermines the experiential value Taxus protects. Six weeks of writing practice between these two framings means students have accumulated evidence — a corpus of fragments, a history of revision, a record of process documentation — with which to evaluate the substitution claim from the inside rather than as abstraction.

The course's tech stack is itself an Acer argument enacted at $30 per student. Note cards substitute structured capture for unstructured memory. Eli Review substitutes a peer feedback platform for ad hoc in-class exchange. Folder naming conventions substitute organizational infrastructure for the labor of finding things later. Each tool is a capital-intensive substitution for a labor-intensive alternative, and the course makes these substitutions _visible_ rather than transparent. The visibility matters: a technology that disappears into background assumption cannot be examined as a design choice, and design choices are where the distribution question lives. The $30 cost is itself a constraint that embodies the definition — affordable capital that democratizes rather than concentrates the substitution, available to any student and any institution willing to invest twenty dollars in index cards and ten in a software subscription.
