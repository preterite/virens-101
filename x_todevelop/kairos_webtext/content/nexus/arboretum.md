## Narration

You are standing at the edge of an open field. To the west, there is a low stone building. The field is densely overgrown. Through it and down, a narrow absence of field marked by gravel leads into a collection of trees--not a forest, not a park. Someone planted these. The spacing and selection are deliberate. Ahead of you, the high grasses to either side fall away as the field's path crosses toward an open space and descends.

A lone oak blooms huge above a mown lawn. The tree's spiral-wreathed leaves crown widely out from the centuried gray-furrowed mass of its core. Acorns in dozens and hundreds dot the lawn toward a pair of trees beyond. To the east, the lawn fades into overgrowth. Past the oak, the pair resolves as imbalanced: vase-shaped, stately. To the west, arranged groups of dark evergreens shape the arboretum's curated space. The oak's lawn ends.

The gravel path descends toward shade. The canopies of two elms arch toward each other, one lower than the other, and for a few strides you are walking through a corridor that once was longer: an _allée_ that has outlived the symmetry it was meant to enforce. The taller tree's bark is dark, braided, channeled in long vertical ridges; the shorter one beside it holds the same vase-shape without the weight. A Princeton cultivar, ten years in the ground. On one side, set slightly apart from the row, a third elm casts different evidence: the lacebark sheds its maps in copper-mottled plates. Leaves gather along the path's edges, one side of each lower than the other, doubly serrate. Things that look balanced aren't. The light through the corridor moves, broken by the canopy and your passage. The elms end. The shaped space thins behind you. 

The gravel dips where the ground softens and the path loses its corridor. Maples close around, taller ones overhead and smaller ones filling in beneath, and for the first time the trees are not ahead or above but beside. The spacing has changed. In the shade their leaves are a range of greens: the heavier, steadier tone of the larger sugar maples and the brighter, eager-looking foliage of smaller red maples planted among them. Your eyes try to decide which trunk to follow upward and give up and return to the level where the trees meet you: bark texture, low branches, the spacings between. Your gaze drops again to the gravel path and registers a curiosity after the acorns. The ground is peppered with early-season samaras, their lobed wing-pairs splayed open. The word lands on something your hand already knows: you've held the dry seed-pod helicopters, spun and watched them flutter in the maple's engineered descent, wings calibrated for different distances from the parent trunk. You scuff your soles and hear their dry, papery tick against the stones. 

As you near the end of the cluster of maples, the open lawn from the commons withdraws its forgiving margins. The boundary arrives in your body before becoming legible; an ecotone announced by contact. Gravel gives way to blue-gray flagstone. On stone you step with intention, seeing seams, noting the dark lines where moss begins to hold. The land's aspect shifts here: a south-facing shelf that holds warmth longer, that feels sheltered as the slope and plantings do the work of a wall.

There's the sense of an entrance toward the next section of trees: the deliberate stone of the path, the thickness of the branches, and what surrounds you resembles invitation. The plantings cease suggesting individual structure and explode into volume. Before color, scent--heavy, sweet, oddly powdery--and then both at once, the smell thickening as the flowers close in. Oleander meets you as a lush, repeated, insistently ornamental mass; a dense presence of coral and cream against deep green that fills the space beside the flagstones and presses color toward you. Leaves repeat in long narrow blades; flower clusters gather and flare in an advertisement of proliferation. Nothing about the space asks you to be careful. Each cluster of flowers echoes the last; each long, leathery leaf feels more cast than grown. Oleander thrives in repetition and its profusion of leaf and bloom. The curated color and density press close.

Further in, the planting tightens. The shelf draws you along and light recedes. The color temperature cools, the air stills, the leaves around you stop catching sun and start absorbing it. The stone underfoot picks up a dampness; moss resurfaces between cracks. The oleander's openness gives way to something older.

The yews arrive as presence before detail: dense, dark bodies that quiet the space. Their gravity dominates. The bark's reddish-brown, scaly surface flakes in irregular patches, revealing purple-toned underbark beneath. And then there is the hollow: a trunk opened so widely it feels less like damage than architecture, an aperture you can peer into. Here the vocabulary becomes necessary. The heartwood, the dense core we imagine as the tree's strength, is gone. What remains is the living margin, the working ring of cambium, a thin persistence that pushes yearly outward. The red arils--fleshy, jewel-like--appear bright against the needles. They look familiar, edible, safe. The yew's living rind and emptied center feel both ancient and uncanny, like a rule you didn't know living things could follow. You step carefully past its hollow body.

Holly catches you not by height but by contact. It occupies the understory in a way that changes your posture, your head and shoulders still aware of the enclosing canopy, but attention drawn down and in, to the level where leaves meet sleeves and berries meet sight. After the yew's deep stillness, holly feels alert: glossy leaves reflect what little light remains, the red drupes held vividly in the semi-dark. Here at hand-height, the berries' redness feels more offered than displayed, and in the still air the oleander's sweetness lingers as though the clearing has kept it. You notice that what draws your hand is exactly what should stop it. The spines are where you expect them: on the reachable leaves, the ones your hand might brush. Higher up, where nothing touches, the edges smooth out beyond where heterophylly permits the plant its defenses.

By the time you're moving past the holly, the flagstone has taken on a second surface: leaf litter gathering in the joints, a softening layer beginning to obscure the path's engineered clarity. The space feels more fully enclosed with the layered insistence of stone underfoot, evergreen around, older darkness above. This part of the arboretum teaches a kind of attention different from that required by the openness of the commons behind you: slower, closer, more cautious. The plants have changed how you hesitate to reach.

The path drops away from the shelf. The change comes overhead first. Light thins. Shadows join. The air cools. Canopy closure edits the ground, and you feel the sky's ceiling lower. The flagstones carry you forward until they don't: where the ground softens past walking, boardwalk replaces stone. The structure answers your weight with hollow sound, plank by plank across saturated land. Water rises. The smell changes from sun-warmed leaf to soil and slow decomposition. Ahead, a planted screen.

Cedar stands in dense ranks, its forms narrow and pyramidal, trained toward architecture. You approach it the way you approach a doorway. The foliage hangs in flattened sprays, fan-like in scaly overlap, fibrous bark peeling in vertical strips. Your shoulder brushes a branch. The volatile compounds release: resinous, immediate, clean. On the other side of the cedars, the boardwalk brings you to water's edge. The space opens laterally into bank and flow. A stream, the continuing sound of moving water, the wet ground between.

Alder occupies the boundary. Its pale and lichen-dusted bark catches what light survives the canopy and reads at a distance before you recognize leaf shape. Closer, lenticels mark the bark in regular pores. The higher branches dangle tiny, persistent strobiles, cone-like and tenacious to outlast seasons of leaf-fall. A pioneer, the alder arrives early and reconfigures the arrival of its successors. It favors difficult ground--wet, unstable, nutrient-poor--and opens that ground over time, changing the mix of the soil. You walk the planks beside the stream. The boardwalk carries you forward over the alder's work.

You step away from a bright, bark-lit margin into the interior. The pale mottling of the alders drops back and the stream's sound stays, but the air begins to thicken with green. A hemlock grove gathers itself around the boardwalk. The light turns uniform and filtered, as if passed through fabric; the understory hushes; the ceiling closes. The scent of evergreen replaces flower and soil. Underfoot, the boards are dusted with scattered needles. Your steps go quiet in the duff. For a passing moment, the place feels perfected. 

You notice light arriving where it shouldn't. Thin shafts cut in low. The lowest hemlock branches, which should be the room's dark drapery, look wrong: sparse needles, bare twigs, an empty ladder climbing from the base's shade. The distant canopy reads as mass; up close, it begins to unlace. You approach a living branch close enough to see its underside and the cause resolves. Tiny white tufts cling to the twigs like caught wool. Past those living white-flecked branches stands a revenant, needles gone and branches rigid, the silhouette more scaffold than canopy. At chest height the bark is interrupted by a clean, circumferential cut too even to be weather, too complete to be accident. No tufts remain on the girdled husk. Beyond the dead tree, the grove takes up its quiet. The other hemlocks hold their perforated canopy, but the filtered light feels now less shelter than interval.

The boardwalk ends abruptly enough that you note your own caution. Wood becomes bare earth and stone. The constructed path withdraws, and your feet negotiate the land directly--uneven, tilting, beginning a steeper drop. The stream falls away. You register another scent. Not the clean release of a brushed hedge, but a richer sweetness. Star anise arrives in the nose before it greets the eye. The tree's glossy leaves emerge first from the ravine's mid-slope, catching what light makes it down through the opening canopy, then the clustered, nodding shapes tucked in close. The radiate geometry of flowers and fruit repeats like an emblem. You bend close toward one of the star-shaped fruits and reward yourself with a drawn breath of licorice and clove and the woody undertone of camphor. 

The ravine changes the rules as you descend. Underfoot, bare earth replaces constructed confidence. Rough stone shows where water has washed the soil thin. Sheltered slope, a bend, an exposed rim where the air feels warmer. The canopy loosens further overhead. Sun finds the path again in patches that feel startling after the wet forest's continuous green.

On the drier, sun-struck rim, the gnarled and fissured olive waits like an abstract sculpture. The tree's form twists, knots, revises. Above, the leaves shimmer with their own two-toned logic--dark gray-green facing the light, silvery beneath, so the crown flickers briefly in the breeze. You look for where the coppice begins: a swelling, a seam, a graft that cuts back and returns.

The rim releases. The aspect tilts again and the path pulls you from the dry ledge toward the ravine's bottom. Rising walls of earth and bole increasingly block the light. In your downward transit, water emerges from feature to condition. The tupelo stands where the ground can't decide between land and pond. Your shoes meet saturation. The trunk rises straight out of standing water with a kind of refusal--vertical, tapering, holding itself up without flourish. At its base, the flare of a buttress begins, a widening that reads more as adaptation than ornament: the tree bracing itself in soft ground. Around you, the ravine walls make a bowl of air and shade, and when you crane your gaze, the sky above is a distant opening, framed by the path's measure of your descent. The tree's branches layer out in tiers, the previous irregularity of the canopy right-angled and deliberate. The bark offers the tupelo's contrasting understatement: thick, blocky alligator-hide, a furrowed surface of plates and ridges that clasp and fasten its weathering. Above, the leaves blaze scarlet, purple, bright orange in their senescent seasonal turn. 

The terrain has brought you to its lowest point. You are standing at the end of the path before a stone pedestal. Around you is a forest. A stream flows down the gully and pools at your feet. On the pedestal rests a large empty iron cauldron, the remaining crumbles and clumps of soil sticking to its seams. Someone planted something here.

In 1634, the Flemish physiologist Jan Baptist van Helmont planted a five-pound sapling in a pot with two hundred pounds of dried soil. He watered and tended the tree for five years. At the end of the experiment's term, he removed the tree and the soil from the pot and weighed them. The soil had diminished by two ounces. The tree had grown to 169 pounds.

The quantitation van Helmont performed missed the medium. He inferred that the tree's increase in mass had come from water rather than air; from hydrogen rather than carbon.
