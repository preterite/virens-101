# Olea (Olive)

## Core Claim

[[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_illicium|Illicium]] established that durable texts circulate and that reading is the activating labor that sets them in motion. But circulation is not neutral passage. Every time a text moves — from writer to peer, from student to instructor, from classroom to institution, from corpus to portfolio — value gets captured, redirected, or transformed. Appropriation names this capture, and it takes diverse forms. The composition classroom is not a special economic zone exempt from appropriation dynamics; it is a site where self-directed, gift, communal, institutional, and market forms of appropriation are already operating simultaneously, and course design distributes who occupies which position. Making these forms visible — tracing who labors, who receives the surplus, and what conditions sustain each arrangement — is the analytical work that converts the webtext's economic framework from description to intervention.

## Theoretical Framework

J.K. Gibson-Graham's diverse economies framework provides the map. The default assumption in composition studies — and in much political-economy-of-composition scholarship — treats the classroom as a site affected by economic forces: adjunctification, assessment regimes, institutional budgets, student debt. The critique operates from outside: economic pressures bear down on writing instruction, and the critical response is to resist, ameliorate, or expose them. Gibson-Graham refuse this framing. Economic activity is not monolithically capitalist, and the relevant question is not how capital exploits the classroom but what diverse economic forms are already operating within it. Market exchange coexists with gift, communal sharing, self-directed production, and institutional allocation — and these forms interact, overlap, and compete for the same material. The composition classroom, on Gibson-Graham's map, is not a protected space that economic forces invade but a diverse economy in its own right.

This is the ontological move that separates the webtext's economic argument from its predecessors. The claim is not that economic metaphors illuminate writing instruction. The claim is that writing instruction _is_ economic activity — production, circulation, appropriation happening in the acts of writing, response, and revision, nameable through the operational vocabulary that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_ulmus|Ulmus]] provides — and that the diverse forms this activity takes are designable. Self-directed appropriation is the form composition pedagogy most needs and least recognizes. When students build a corpus they control, manage, and deploy for their own purposes — when the accumulated textual capital that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_nerium|Nerium]] describes remains available to the writer as a resource rather than being extracted by assessment — the student is appropriating her own labor. The corpus is personal capital. Revision is self-investment. The fragment model, the dev log, the portfolio assembled from accumulated material: these are infrastructure for self-directed appropriation, and positioning them as such reframes the entire political economy of the classroom. Students are not only objects of institutional extraction; they can be appropriators of their own labor.

Gift appropriation operates through peer feedback. A reviewer's labor produces value — the response, the evaluation, the suggestion — that returns to the writer without market exchange. Citation practices participate in the same economy: to cite is to acknowledge what one received, to declare a dependency that is also a gift. The gift form is not costless — [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_tsuga|Tsuga]]'s dependency analysis applies — but the structure of the exchange differs from both market and institutional forms. No price is paid. No credential is exchanged. Something of value nevertheless moves.

Communal appropriation names the shared knowledge that no individual produced and no individual owns: collaboratively developed norms, the commons of genre conventions, the class's accumulated understanding of what works and why. This form is infrastructural in the sense that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_ilex|Ilex]] describes — it becomes visible primarily when it fails, when the commons is depleted or when norms that seemed shared turn out to be contested.

Institutional appropriation is the form composition studies knows best because it is the form the field has critiqued most extensively. Assessment converts process into credential. Credit hours quantify pedagogical labor as exchangeable units. Outcome metrics extract from the classroom's diverse activity a standardized representation of value that serves institutional rather than student purposes. The critique is real, but treating institutional appropriation as the only form — or as the form against which all others are measured — reproduces the monolithic economic lens Gibson-Graham refuse.

Market appropriation operates at the course's horizon: the eventual circulation of student competence as labor-market value, the professional writing that transforms classroom learning into economic return. This form matters less within the semester than the others, but it matters to students, and its anticipated presence shapes what they value in the course. A student who understands that her accumulated corpus is capital — deployable beyond the semester, available for future appropriation in professional and academic contexts — relates to the work differently than one who understands the semester as a series of performances assessed and discarded.

Stephen Resnick and Richard Wolff's class-analytic framework provides the complement to Gibson-Graham's map. Where Gibson-Graham identifies which forms of appropriation coexist, Resnick and Wolff trace the internal structure within any single form: who performs the labor, who receives the surplus, and what conditions — cultural, political, institutional, economic — sustain or disrupt that arrangement. Resnick and Wolff define class not as a group identity but as a process: the production, appropriation, and distribution of surplus labor. Any economic arrangement, including a pedagogical one, can be analyzed by asking these three questions.

Applied to the composition classroom, the questions become precise. In peer feedback (gift appropriation): students labor to produce responses; the receiving writer captures the surplus as material for revision; the conditions sustaining the arrangement include assignment design, trust, time allocation, and the permission structures that [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_thuja|Thuja]] names. Change the conditions — require feedback but don't allocate class time; assess feedback quality rather than completion — and the internal structure shifts. The gift form may persist nominally while operating more like institutional extraction in practice. The analysis is pedagogically actionable because the conditions are designable: if you can trace the internal structure of an appropriation form, you can redesign the conditions that sustain it.

Bruce Horner's materialist critique provides the disciplinary warrant for treating appropriation as a composition studies concern rather than an imported one. Attending to the material conditions of writing labor, Horner insists, includes attending to who benefits from each stage of the production-circulation-appropriation cycle. The webtext's framework extends Horner's insistence in the direction Gibson-Graham makes available: the benefits are not uniformly extractive, the beneficiaries are not only institutions, and the pedagogical designer's leverage lies in the distribution of forms rather than in the resistance to a single dominant one. The raw material of student writing, like any raw material, requires transformation before it yields value — and the form of that transformation determines who benefits. The graft that joins a wild rootstock to a cultivated variety produces fruit neither could produce alone, but the question of who harvests, who presses, and who trades remains open at every stage.

## Course Implementation

VIRENS 101 distributes appropriation deliberately across the semester's constraint sequence. The design principle is not to eliminate institutional appropriation — the course exists within an institution and produces grades — but to ensure that self-directed, gift, and communal forms have sufficient infrastructure to operate alongside institutional extraction rather than being crowded out by it.

The fragment model is self-directed appropriation infrastructure. Students accumulate textual capital in their own corpus, organized by their own systems, available for their own purposes beyond the semester. The corpus does not belong to the institution. The grade assesses the student's management and deployment of the corpus, but the corpus itself — the accumulated material, the fragment archive, the dev log record — remains the student's property. This is a design decision with economic consequences: it means the student's labor produces capital that the student retains, and the institutional assessment operates on a representation of that capital rather than consuming the capital itself. Cut to the ground, the source regrows from its own roots — the student's corpus survives the semester's harvest because the harvest was never designed to take the roots.

The W–P–R rhythm distributes gift appropriation across the semester. Every peer review cycle creates a gift exchange: labor produces feedback, feedback circulates to the writer, the writer's revision incorporates the gift into new production. Eli Review's feedback analytics make the gift economy partially visible — students can see the circulation of their labor as reviewers and the returns their reviews generate for peers — but the infrastructure also constrains the form: structured prompts, required response patterns, and completion tracking ensure that the gift exchange operates reliably rather than depending on goodwill alone. [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/content/nexus/_alnus|Alnus]]'s constraint sequencing shapes when each appropriation form becomes available. Early in the semester, self-directed accumulation dominates: students generate material without external evaluation. Gift exchange enters in Movement 2 as peer review arrives. Institutional appropriation is deferred to Movement 4, when the portfolio freezes the stock for evaluative display. The sequence is the distribution: by the time institutional assessment operates on the corpus, self-directed and gift forms have had fourteen weeks to build value that assessment cannot fully extract. The time-shifting that Nerium describes — deferring assessment until the stock reaches threshold — is also an appropriation-distribution strategy. Early assessment concentrates institutional capture before other forms have had time to operate. Late assessment allows the student's self-directed appropriation to compound.

The portfolio introduction performs the moment at which the student narrates her own appropriation history. Which forms of value capture operated on her work across the semester? Where did her labor go? What did she retain? The reflective component is not merely metacognitive — it is the student's first exercise in economic self-awareness as a writer, the recognition that writing labor produces value whose distribution is partially visible and partially designable. This is the analytical capacity that the eleven preceding nexus pages have been building toward: the writer who can see not only what she wrote and how, but who captured what and under what conditions.
