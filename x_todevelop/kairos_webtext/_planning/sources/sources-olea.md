# Olea Sources: Appropriation and Value

Position 11 in the QUANTITATION sequence. Scenes G4, V3.

The Olea nexus receives the diverse-appropriation claim from Illicium (#10) and develops it: if circulation takes diverse forms, then so does appropriation. Two complementary analytical frameworks do the work — Gibson-Graham maps the diversity of appropriation forms, Resnick and Wolff trace the internal structure within any single form. The nexus page is where the webtext's economic argument becomes explicitly political.

---

## Claim 1: Appropriation Takes Diverse Forms

**Core formulation:** If circulation takes diverse forms, then so does appropriation — the process by which circulating value gets captured, redirected, or transformed. Appropriation is not monolithic extraction but varies across self-directed, gift, communal, institutional, and market forms.

**Source:** Gibson-Graham, J.K. *A Postcapitalist Politics*. University of Minnesota Press, 2006. Also *The End of Capitalism (As We Knew It)*. University of Minnesota Press, 1996/2006.

**What Gibson-Graham does:** The diverse economies framework refuses the assumption that capitalism is a monolithic totality. Economic activity includes market and non-market forms, paid and unpaid labor, capitalist and non-capitalist enterprise — and these coexist, overlap, and interact. Gibson-Graham provides the *map*: a way of seeing economic diversity where a monolithic lens sees only extraction.

**What the Olea nexus does with Gibson-Graham:** Applies the diverse economies framework to the composition classroom specifically. The five-form enumeration — self-directed, gift, communal, institutional, market — names what actually happens when student writing circulates:

- **Self-directed:** Students appropriating their own labor (the corpus as personal capital; revision as self-investment). Leads the enumeration because it's the most disruptive form for composition studies — students can be the appropriators of their own labor, not just the objects of institutional extraction.
- **Gift:** Peer feedback that returns value to the writer without market exchange. Citation as gift economy (acknowledging what one received).
- **Communal:** Shared classroom knowledge, collaboratively developed norms, the commons of genre conventions available to all participants.
- **Institutional:** Assessment, credentialing, credit hours — the university's capture of pedagogical labor as exchangeable units.
- **Market:** Publication, professional writing, the eventual circulation of student competence as labor-market value.

**Why self-directed leads:** Session digest 2026-02-17b established the rationale: self-appropriation is the most disruptive form because it counters the default assumption in composition studies that students are objects of institutional extraction. Positioning students as potential appropriators of their own labor reframes the entire political economy of the classroom.

**Status:** ✅ Gibson-Graham in foundational sources and Project Knowledge context.

## Claim 2: Class Analysis Traces Internal Structure

**Core formulation:** Where Gibson-Graham maps the diversity of appropriation forms, class analysis traces who performs the labor, who receives the surplus, and what conditions sustain or disrupt that arrangement within any single form.

**Source:** Resnick, Stephen A., and Richard D. Wolff. *Knowledge and Class: A Marxian Critique of Political Economy*. University of Chicago Press, 1987. Also *New Departures in Marxian Theory*. Routledge, 2006.

**What Resnick and Wolff do:** Develop an overdeterminist Marxian class analysis where "class" names not a group identity but a *process* — the production, appropriation, and distribution of surplus labor. Any economic arrangement can be analyzed by asking: who labors, who receives the surplus, and what conditions (cultural, political, natural, economic) sustain or disrupt that arrangement?

**What the Olea nexus does with Resnick and Wolff:** Provides the finer-grained analytical tool that Gibson-Graham's map needs. Gibson-Graham tells you *which forms* of appropriation coexist; Resnick and Wolff tell you *what's happening inside* any one form. Peer feedback (gift appropriation) still involves labor and surplus — someone worked to produce the feedback, someone receives it — and the conditions sustaining that arrangement (assignment design, trust, time allocation) are designable. This is where the nexus becomes pedagogically actionable: if you can trace the internal structure of an appropriation form, you can redesign the conditions.

**Division of labor (architectural decision, session 2026-02-17c):** Gibson-Graham is explicitly attributed in the Olea tree narrative. Resnick and Wolff are positioned as the complement. The two are not redundant — they operate at different scales of analysis (map vs. internal structure), and each has distinct analytical work to do on the nexus page.

**Status:** ✅ Resnick and Wolff identified. In reading pipeline but not yet closely engaged for specific passages.

## Claim 3: Pedagogical Design Distributes Appropriation

**Core formulation:** The pedagogical question becomes which forms of appropriation operate in a given composing situation and who occupies which positions — and course design can redistribute these.

**Sources:**

**Edwards (2014):** The composition classroom is a site where diverse economic activity is already occurring. Pedagogical design doesn't *introduce* economic dynamics; it makes existing ones visible and intervenes in their distribution.

**Horner, Bruce.** *Terms of Work for Composition*. SUNY Press, 2000. Horner's materialist critique foregrounds the material conditions under which writing labor occurs and the institutional structures that appropriate its value. Provides disciplinary warrant for treating appropriation as a composition studies concern rather than an imported one.

**Supporting:** The *four-constellations* reasons file (listed in tree narrative sources) documents how different pedagogical arrangements — workshop, seminar, studio, lecture — distribute appropriation differently. The constellation framework gives concrete shape to the abstract claim that design distributes appropriation.

**What this claim does:** Connects the analytical apparatus (Gibson-Graham + Resnick/Wolff) to the webtext's design argument. The Olea nexus is where the twelve principles' political implications become explicit: constraint sequencing (#8) shapes who gets to appropriate value at each stage; operational vocabulary (#2) makes the appropriation visible; the experience good argument (#5) explains why some appropriation forms destroy value while others don't.

**Status:** ✅ Edwards and Horner in foundational sources. Four-constellations reasons file exists.

## Open Questions

- **Resnick and Wolff close reading** — The source is identified and the analytical role is clear, but specific passages for the nexus page have not been selected. The key concept is their process definition of class (who labors, who receives surplus, what sustains the arrangement). Urgency: medium. The framework is well-known enough to deploy in general terms, but the nexus page will be stronger with specific textual engagement.

- **Watkins on derivative value** — Shared open question with Illicium. The derivative-value argument (composition derives value from elsewhere; this framework repositions it as producing value through organized circulation) could ground the Olea nexus's political argument. If Illicium establishes the circulation premise and Olea develops the appropriation consequence, Watkins would connect both to the institutional politics of English departments. Urgency: medium.

- **"Appropriation" as term** — Flagged in frag-18 (2026-01-26): does "appropriation" carry negative connotations that work against the diverse-economies argument? Gibson-Graham's framework deliberately includes positive forms (self-directed, gift), but the word itself may trigger resistance in a composition studies audience accustomed to hearing "appropriation" as exploitation. The nexus page may need to address this directly — perhaps in the opening paragraph, naming the connotation and then showing why diversity of form is the point.

- **V3 scene coordination with Alnus** — Both nexuses use V3. The productive reading: Vera's design reflection operates simultaneously as constraint-sequencing analysis (Alnus) and appropriation-distribution analysis (Olea). The nexus pages should acknowledge the shared scene and direct readers to the other lens. This is a feature of the hypertext structure, not a conflict to resolve.
