# 1. Q — Quercus (Labor and Capital + Quantitation as Rhetoric)

**Scenes**: V1, G3 | **Sources**: `writing-labor-capital`

The argument begins with its foundational wager: when writers attend to how information moves and who appropriates textual value, they reveal conditions under which writing labor transforms into capital—and can then act on those conditions. Writing-as-verb and writing-as-noun name two phases of a single economic process: human effort creates textual artifacts, and textual artifacts inform and enable future human effort. Quantitation enters here as the rhetorical operation that makes this transformation countable and therefore visible: how much writing, how many drafts, what accumulates. The scenes at this position (V1 establishing design rationale, G3 encountering the framework) introduce readers to the visualization—an economic vocabulary for tracking what happens as texts move through composing, sharing, reading, and remaking.

# 2. U — Ulmus (Six Moves vs. Writing Process)

**Scenes**: V2, G2 | **Sources**: `six-moves-vocabulary`, `six-moves-process-pedagogy`

With labor-capital dynamics established, the argument turns to how writers name what they do. Traditional writing process pedagogy names temporal stages (prewriting → drafting → revising → editing → publishing); the six moves vocabulary names the operations writers perform on information—notice (capture), sort (classify), direct (route), compress (distill), link (connect), release into circulation (publish). The operational vocabulary works in addition to the temporal one, not as a replacement: process pedagogy describes when writers work, while six moves describes what writers do with information at each stage. Naming operations makes them distinguishable, and once distinguishable they become specifiable—able to carry further attributes like proportion and relation—and therefore available for deliberate sequencing beyond temporal succession. This nexus offers a rationale for integrating systems thinking: not to replace process pedagogy but to make visible what it sometimes obscures about how text and attention interact. The early-semester scene positions reflect where vocabulary encounter actually happens: Vera designing the framework's introduction (V2) and Grant first encountering an operational vocabulary alongside the process language he arrived with (G2).

# 3. A — Acer (Economy and Technology in Composition)

**Scenes**: G1, V4 | **Sources**: [[sources-acer]]

Questions raised by labor-capital dynamics point toward a definition: technology substitutes capital (or capital-intensive processes) for labor (or labor-intensive processes). Writing is a technology in precisely this sense—orthographic systems, genres, citation conventions all represent accumulated solutions that reduce the cognitive labor of future writers. If writing is a technology, then the economic processes revealed by attending to its labor-capital cycle are not imported from another domain but intrinsic to composition itself. The study of writing instruction becomes a suitable and necessary domain for economic inquiry. Grant's opening-week scene (G1) foregrounds the differentials of the course's tech stack—note cards, Eli Review, folder structures—making the technological substrate of writing visible as accumulated capital before he writes a word. Vera's mid-semester reflection (V4) recognizes that the technologies she chose for the course are not neutral delivery mechanisms but capital-intensive substitutions for labor-intensive processes: the definitional point enacted through design.

# 4. N — Nerium (Textual Accumulation + Archives Give Analytics)

**Scenes**: V5, G5, G6 | **Sources**: `textual-accumulation`

Economic inquiry into composition examines both pedagogical processes (learning by doing, writing-as-verb) and the accumulated textual capital that permits reconfiguration for future writing (writing-as-noun). Textual accumulation names the deliberate pedagogical strategy of building archives that students can draw upon—not merely saving drafts but creating accessible repositories of prior work. Archives give analytics: the accumulated text becomes analyzable, revealing patterns invisible in any single piece. This nexus connects labor (the scenes show Grant's experience of accumulating work across the semester) with the capital that accumulation produces (Vera's design for making archives pedagogically productive). The absorption of "archives give analytics" into this position strengthens the connection between storage and analysis.

# 5. T₁ — Taxus (YOLO Up, Constrain Down)

**Scenes**: V1, G1 | **Sources**: `yolo-up-constrain-down`

Generation produces value that cannot be previewed, outsourced, or recovered once foreclosed—it must be experienced to yield its returns. Composition pedagogy has long protected generative space (freewriting, brainstorming, invention heuristics), and systems thinkers share this intuition: early-stage design promotes maximum possibility before refinement arrives. But the economic framing sharpens the rationale. Protecting generation from premature evaluation is not pedagogical indulgence but economic necessity: the labor of generation operates as an experience good whose value is structurally destroyed by interruption. "YOLO up, constrain down" names this temporal sequence as itself a constraint to be designed, recovering Peter Elbow's generative pedagogy against social-turn critiques by grounding it in the structure of the labor rather than the psychology of the writer. Scenes V1 and G1 position this principle at the course's opening—both designer and student encounter the same logic from different vantages.

# 6. I — Ilex (Infrastructure Visibility + Difficulty-as-Diagnostic)

**Scenes**: G2, V5 | **Sources**: infrastructure portion of old Tsuga; absorbs difficulty-as-diagnostic

The experience of difficulty can be a diagnostic illuminating the presence and nature of constraints otherwise invisible. Following Susan Leigh Star, infrastructure becomes visible primarily upon breakdown—the systems that support writing (technological, generic, institutional) reveal themselves when they fail to function smoothly. Folding difficulty-as-diagnostic into infrastructure visibility strengthens both concepts: difficulty is not deficiency but signal, and infrastructure study requires attention to where things go wrong. Grant's first encounter with genuine friction (G2) exposes infrastructure he did not know existed—the gap between what he expected writing in college to feel like and the systems actually operating on his work. Vera's later design reflections (V5) reframe that same friction as productive information, demonstrating how pedagogical difficulty can be read rather than remediated. The temporal separation between Grant's experience and Vera's analysis performs the diagnostic logic: difficulty arrives first, understanding follows.

# 7. T₂ — Thuja (Permission Flags)

**Scenes**: V6, G6 | **Sources**: `permission-flags`

Writers develop agency not by escaping constraints but by learning to analyze them. Constraints vary along identifiable dimensions—visibility, rationale, alterability, ownership—and the same constraint can function differently depending on its properties: a formatting requirement controlled by an instructor operates differently from one controlled by an academic journal, even if the requirement itself is identical. The UNIX concept of permission flags (who can read, write, or execute) offers a heuristic for thinking about these variable properties, but the core claim is about writer capacity, not system design. Writers who learn to assess constraint properties can focus action on the intervening differences—recognizing what is genuinely immovable, understanding rationale where ownership is unavailable, and claiming ownership where appropriate. This nexus moves toward constraint sequencing (position 8) by establishing that constraints have not just content but analyzable properties that writers can learn to navigate. The late-semester scene positions are deliberate: by V6, Vera can reflect on how the course supported development of constraint literacy across the full arc, and by G6, Grant can recognize retrospectively the analytical trajectory he has lived through—from encountering constraints as opaque requirements to assessing their properties and acting on the differences.

# 8. A₂ — Alnus (Constraint Sequencing)

**Scenes**: V1, V3, G3 | **Sources**: `constraint-sequencing`

Sequences will shape rewards gained from following them; one useful strategy is to determine what constraints are present and then sequence their arrival to allow for maximum benefit gained from human labor. Constraint sequencing names the central pedagogical design principle: not which constraints to impose but when they arrive and what handles them. This is the article's primary theoretical contribution—a framework for thinking about temporal design in composition pedagogy. The multiple Vera scenes (V1, V3) show constraint sequencing as a design problem; G3 shows it as a student experience of encountering constraints in deliberate order. This position sits at the center of the QUANTITATION sequence, reflecting its centrality to the overall argument.

# 9. T₃ — Tsuga (Citation as Dependency)

**Scenes**: G3, V5 | **Sources**: `citation-as-dependency`

Technological innovations that help answer questions about constraint sequencing include citations—in the conventional library sense, they allow one to trace sequences of post-publication academic argument, but in the local sense they function as pointers, notes, or hyperlinks connecting ideas. Constraint sequencing and the six moves make use of this second sense: citations as dependency declarations that say "these are related" or "this needs X and Y to succeed." Citations carry context and carry the dependencies of arguments, making visible what any given text requires to function. This repositions citation instruction from compliance with academic convention toward understanding how texts announce and manage their conceptual dependencies. Grant's mid-semester scene (G3) shows citation dependency as a student encounter—the moment when sources stop being compliance objects and start functioning as structural requirements. Vera's reflection (V5) analyzes the same shift from the designer's perspective, connecting citation infrastructure to the broader visibility argument developed in Ilex (position 6).

# 10. I₂ — Illicium (Circulation)

**Scenes**: G2, V4, G6 | **Sources**: `circulation`, `writing-labor-capital`

Citations as dependency networks help track the time-based stages of economic circulation that draw on labor and capital: production, distribution, use, and reproduction. But circulation can only be tracked because writing produces durable artifacts — texts persist in ways that spoken exchange does not, and that persistence makes the movement of value _traceable_ rather than merely present. Following John Trimbur, circulation is not merely what happens after writing but a dynamic that shapes writing at every stage, operating microeconomically (within drafts, between writer and peer) as well as macroeconomically (across disciplinary publication and institutional exchange). The scenes at this position trace how circulation awareness develops across the course: G2 shows Grant's early encounter with circulation as something that is already happening to his work before he notices it; V4 shows Vera adjusting course design in response to how material is actually moving; G6 shows end-of-term synthesis, where the full arc of circulation becomes retrospectively visible. This nexus connects backward to citation dependency and forward to appropriation — if durable texts circulate, then value moves through diverse channels, and the question becomes what forms that appropriation takes and who participates at each stage.

# 11. O — Olea (Appropriation and Value)

**Scenes**: G4, V3 | **Sources**: `circulation`, `writing-labor-capital`, `four-constellations`

If circulation takes diverse forms, then so does appropriation — the process by which circulating value gets captured, redirected, or transformed. The diverse economies framework (Gibson-Graham) provides the analytical apparatus: appropriation is not monolithic extraction but varies across self-directed, gift, communal, institutional, and market forms. The pedagogical question becomes which forms operate in a given composing situation and who occupies which positions. Grant's mid-to-late semester scene (G4) shows the moment when accumulated labor becomes visible as something others capture — instructor evaluation, institutional metrics, peer use — and when the _form_ of that capture starts to matter: peer feedback that returns value to the writer operates differently from institutional assessment that extracts it. Vera's mid-semester design adjustment (V3) reveals the designer grappling with distributional outcomes: are students appropriating what she intended, or is the institution capturing something she didn't design for? The shared V3 scene with Alnus (constraint sequencing) is productive: because the sequence in which constraints arrive shapes who gets to appropriate value at each stage, the same design reflection analyzed through both lenses yields genuine insight about the relationship between temporal design and distributional outcomes. Resnick and Wolff's class-analytic framework offers the finer-grained tool here — where Gibson-Graham maps the diversity of appropriation forms, class analysis traces who performs the labor, who receives the surplus, and what conditions sustain or disrupt that arrangement within any single form.

# 12. N₂ — Nyssa (Corpse, Compost, Garden)

**Scenes**: G5, G6 | **Sources**: `corpse-compost-garden`

The cycle of appropriation leads into the final composting metaphor: growth from corpse to soil for seed to seedling to bush to branch, with both tending-labor and producing-labor, both self-appropriating and other-appropriating behaviors. The metaphor refuses the binary between creation and destruction—dead text becomes substrate for new growth, failed drafts nourish future writing. This position returns to the labor-capital dynamic of position 1 but transformed: where Quercus established the cycle abstractly, Nyssa shows it as organic process with death built in. Grant's late-semester scenes (G5, G6) demonstrate how accumulated textual capital becomes available for composting—not preserved intact but decomposed and reconstituted. The sequence closes by opening: corpse-compost-garden implies continuation beyond any single semester.

# 1. Writing as Economic Cycle

## Claim
When writers attend to the movement of information and the appropriation of textual value, they reveal the conditions and processes under which writing's labor transforms into capital, and thereby facilitate the analysis, sequencing, and revision of those conditions and processes.

## Elaboration
Technologies substitute capital for labor. Writing is a technology. Every act of writing therefore involves economic processes whose stages can be identified and acted upon. When writers attend to such processes, they can map a cycle of production, distribution, use, and reproduction that describes what happens as texts move through composing, sharing, reading, and remaking. The tools and vocabulary of economic analysis—labor, capital, value, appropriation—become applicable in the act of writing. Questions about who benefits from the cycle, and at what stages, become answerable in structural and political terms.

# 2. Operational Vocabulary

## Claim
Naming the operations writers perform on information, in addition to the temporal stages writers move through, makes those operations distinguishable, specifiable, and available for deliberate sequencing.

## Elaboration
Traditional process vocabulary describes temporal stages: prewriting, drafting, revising. These terms name _when_ writers work but obscure _what_ writers do with information at each stage. An operational vocabulary names the work: notice (capture), sort (classify), direct (route), compress (distill), link (connect), release into circulation (publish). These operations overlap in practice but remain distinct as processes. Once named, operations can carry further specifications (proportion, relation) that enable deliberate sequencing beyond temporal succession.

# 3. Textual Accumulation

## Claim
Textual accumulation transforms student writing from discrete assessed performances into analyzable capital available for future reconfiguration.

## Elaboration
Writing pedagogy that treats each assignment as a freestanding performance discards the residue of past labor. A student who writes five essays has five grades; a student who accumulates a corpus of fragments, drafts, and revisions has material that compounds. Accumulation means past writing remains available as input to future writing: an early fragment supplies a claim for a later argument; a failed draft reveals a structural problem that informs the next attempt. The shift from performance to accumulation is itself economic—it is the difference between spending and saving, between labor consumed in the moment of assessment and labor stored as capital. Because writing substitutes accumulated solutions for present cognitive effort, a deliberately managed corpus reduces the cognitive cost of each successive composing act.

# 4. Generation Before Evaluation

## Claim
Maximizing early possibility before introducing constraint creates conditions for accumulation that cannot be achieved by frontloading requirements.

## Elaboration
Generative writing pedagogies share a structural principle with systems engineering: expand the solution space before narrowing it. Introducing evaluative constraints too early truncates the range of material available for later refinement, and that truncation is irreversible—you cannot recover possibilities that were never generated. The principle is not permissiveness but sequence: constraints arrive, but they arrive after the material they will act upon exists. Generation working against minimal constraint encounters resistance—topics that don't develop, moves that stall, methods that produce nothing usable—and that resistance is itself informational. The resistance reveals which directions are productive before the writer has committed to any of them.

# 5. Generation as Experience Good

## Claim
The labor of generation must, as a matter of economic necessity, be protected from evaluative constraint.

## Elaboration
Requiring writers to justify their generative choices during generation forecloses value that cannot be recovered, because the experience that would have produced it did not occur. A writer following felt sense through an early draft cannot know in advance what the draft will yield, because the value of the generative act is constituted by experience. Generation operates in economic terms as an experience good: a category of labor whose value is structurally destroyed by externalization or preemptive assessment.

# 6. Difficulty as Diagnostic

## Claim
Writing difficulty functions as diagnostic signal, exposing infrastructural constraints that remain invisible until a writer pushes against them.

## Elaboration
Difficulty is not noise in the composing process; it is information about the system the writer operates within. When generation stalls, the stall locates a constraint—a gap in knowledge, a structural problem in the argument, a mismatch between the writer's intentions and the available material. These constraints are infrastructural in the sense that they are embedded in the writing situation itself rather than imposed by external evaluation. They become visible only through the encounter: a writer who never attempts the argument never discovers the gap. Treating difficulty as diagnostic rather than as failure changes the pedagogical response from remediation to investigation—the question shifts from "what did the writer do wrong?" to "what does this difficulty reveal about the constraints operating here?"

# 7. Constraint Modes and Ownership

## Claim
Writers develop agency by learning to assess the variable properties of diverse constraints and focus action upon the intervening differences.

## Elaboration
Not all constraints operate the same way. Constraints vary along identifiable dimensions—visibility, rationale, alterability, ownership—and the same constraint can function differently depending on its properties. A formatting requirement controlled by an instructor operates differently from one controlled by an academic journal, even if the requirement itself is identical. Making these properties explicit transforms constraints from background conditions into objects of analysis. Writers who learn to identify constraint properties can negotiate strategically by recognizing which constraints are genuinely immovable, understanding rationale where ownership isn't available, and claiming ownership where appropriate. Composing contexts may impose demands upon writers for increased analytical capacity over decreased rhetorical constraint.

# 8. Constraint Arrival

## Claim
The temporal sequence in which constraints arrive shapes learning outcomes more decisively than the content of those constraints.

## Elaboration
Two courses with identical constraint sets—same formatting requirements, same citation expectations, same rubric criteria—will produce different learning outcomes if the constraints arrive in different sequences. Front-loading all constraints simultaneously creates cognitive overload that inhibits generation; introducing them progressively creates a rhythm of expansion and refinement that supports accumulation. This principle extends beyond individual assignments to course-level design: early weeks operate under minimal constraint to build corpus; middle weeks introduce organizational and citational constraints as the material they act upon accumulates; late weeks layer evaluative and presentational constraints onto material that has already undergone multiple transformations. The sequence is the design. Changing the content of a constraint matters less than changing when it arrives.

# 9. Citation as Dependency

## Claim
Citations declare the dependencies a text requires to succeed and trace the sequence through which value gets appropriated across stages of production.

## Elaboration
Citation is conventionally taught as attribution—giving credit to sources. But citation also functions as dependency management: each citation declares that this text requires that text in order to do its work, just as a software module declares its dependencies on other modules. The dependency framing makes visible what the attribution framing obscures: that a text is a node in a network of value circulation, not a freestanding object that happens to acknowledge its influences. Tracing citations as dependencies reveals the economic structure of knowledge production—which texts enable which other texts, where value accumulates, and where it gets extracted. Teaching citation as dependency rather than attribution shifts the pedagogical emphasis from compliance (did you cite correctly?) to analysis (what does your text need, and where does that need come from?).

# 10. Reading as Productive Consumption

## Claim
Reading is the activating labor that sets inert textual capital in motion, completing one cycle of appropriation while generating material that enters the next.

## Elaboration
A text that is not read is information without use-value: potential meaning that has not been realized. Reading operates as productive consumption by performing the activation that transforms textual capital from inert storage into circulating value. Like generation, reading labor is an experience good: a text's value to its reader cannot be determined before the encounter and cannot be extracted without it. Reading's activating labor produces responses for input to the next cycle of writing. The cycle of production, distribution, use, and reproduction therefore also operates as a cycle of writing, sharing, reading, and rewriting. Reading and writing are two forms of the same transformative work, distinguished by their positions in the circulation sequence.

# 11. Circulation

## Claim
Tracing the circulation of textual value helps writers identify and deliberately participate in the diverse forms of appropriation that occur whenever texts move.

## Elaboration
Motion requires a durable artifact to trace and act upon. Writing's inscriptive durability makes textual circulation possible. While accumulation without circulation is hoarding, and circulation without accumulation is dissipation, both are necessary: texts persist as artifacts whose movements through the stages of circulation can be traced and acted upon. Such motion operates as the necessary site of value appropriation by various participants in writing. The operational vocabulary names what writers do with information at each stage and uncovers where value changes hands and what forms appropriation takes: self-directed, gift, communal, institutional, market. Recognizing this diversity poses structural and political questions about who benefits at each stage, and under what conditions future production remains possible.

# 12. Decomposition Over Preservation

## Claim
Textual decomposition promotes future writing growth more effectively than textual preservation: the corpse of one text becomes compost for the next.

## Elaboration
Publication preserves completed texts into fixed capital, available for reference but resistant to reconfiguration. A text decomposed into its elements returns those elements to the corpus as aspects available for reassembly. Decomposition separates textual operations--claims to re-route, evidence to reclassify, structures to template future work--from the experiential labor that produced their configuration. The separated elements recirculate as liquid capital, no longer locked into a single constraint sequence and available for reinvestment across future sequences. A dead organism that remains intact persists but does not nourish; one that decomposes feeds new growth. Pedagogies that emphasize preservation--the polished essay archived as a finished product--foreclose the generative cycle. Pedagogies that emphasize decomposition--the portfolio disassembled into fragments that re-enter the corpus--keep the cycle turning.

When writers attend to the movement of information and the appropriation of textual value, they reveal an economic cycle: the transformation between writing labor and textual capital whose stages can be identified and acted upon. Traditional process vocabulary describes this cycle's temporal stages (prewriting, drafting, revising) but obscures the operational relationships between human attention and textual material. Naming the operations writers perform on information, in addition to the temporal stages writers move through, makes those operations distinguishable, specifiable, and available for deliberate sequencing. Because writing substitutes accumulated solutions for present cognitive effort, the tools and vocabulary of economic analysis become applicable in the act of writing itself. Deliberate pedagogical design can exploit this dynamic: textual accumulation transforms student writing from discrete assessed performances into analyzable capital available for future reconfiguration.

Generative writing pedagogies create conditions for such accumulation by enacting a principle shared with systems engineering: maximize early possibility, then use constraint to refine. Clearing visible constraints from the workspace opens space for generation; generation, working against what remains, encounters resistance that reveals which topics, moves, or methods might yield the greatest rewards. Writing difficulty thus functions as diagnostic signal, exposing infrastructural constraints that remain invisible until a writer pushes against them. Constraints themselves vary along identifiable dimensions—visibility, rationale, alterability, ownership—and writers develop agency by learning to assess these properties and focus action upon the intervening differences. The temporal sequence in which constraints arrive shapes learning outcomes more decisively than the content of those constraints.

Constraint sequencing requires tools for tracking what each text depends on and where value moves across stages of production. Citations serve both functions: they declare the dependencies a text requires to succeed, and they trace the sequence through which value gets appropriated—production, distribution, use, and reproduction. Tracing the circulation of textual value through these stages helps writers identify and deliberately participate in the diverse forms of appropriation—self-directed, gift, communal, institutional, market—that occur whenever texts move. Textual decomposition promotes future writing growth more effectively than textual preservation: the corpse of one text becomes compost for the next.
