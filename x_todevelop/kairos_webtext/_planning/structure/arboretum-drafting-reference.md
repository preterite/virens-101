### Cross-Cutting Threads

- **Bole**: oak (massive, centuries-accumulated) → yew (hollow) → tupelo (alligator-hide). Three uses across the walk; vertical presence.
- **Cambium call-and-response**: yew (survival — "living cambium around nothing") → hemlock (severance — girdling ring where the living layer was cut). Two fates for the same tissue separated by the cedar-alder passage.
- **Volatile**: cedar (aromatic spray, environmental) → star anise (fragrance as arrival). Scent bookends Group 3.
- **Deadly/unmarked**: oleander, yew, holly all toxic; none announce it. Star anise culinary/toxic distinction by species.
- **Experiential arc**: familiar → enclosed/dangerous → immersive/systemic → descended/transformed.
- **Vocabulary density arc**: sparse → thickening → peak at alder → slight thinning → culminating weight at tupelo.
- **Path surface arc**: gravel → flagstone → boardwalk → bare earth/stone → saturated ground.

### OPENING FRAME

**IF echo:** Paraphrases Zork (1980): "You are standing in an open field west of a white house." Derivative first; progenitor (Adventure) at closing. Inverted chronology enacts dependency-tracing argument. **Enacted, never named.**

**Current working text:**

> You are standing at the edge of an open field. To the west, there is a low stone building. The field is densely overgrown. Through it and down, an absence of field leads into a collection of trees—not a forest, not a park. Someone planted these. The spacing and selection are deliberate. Ahead of you, the high grasses to either side fall away as the gravel path crosses toward an open space and descends.
> 
> A lone oak blooms huge above a mown lawn. The tree's spiral-wreathed leaves spread widely from the centuries-accumulated gray-furrowed mass of its core. Acorns dot the lawn toward a pair of trees beyond. To the east, the lawn fades back into overgrowth. The pair of trees resolves as imbalanced: vase-shaped, stately, one taller than the other. To the west, arranged groups of other species shape the arboretum's curated space.
> 
> The oak's lawn ends. The path's slope inclines into shade toward the lopsided pair. The canopies of two elms arch toward each other, one lower than the other.

**Editorial notes (unapplied):**
1. "Path's absence of field" — concept strong, possessive grammar backward-parsing
2. "Centuries-accumulated gray-furrowed mass" — three stacked compound modifiers; separate visual from temporal
3. "To the west, arranged groups..." — reports rather than perceives; replace with visual impression
4. "Slope inclines" — redundant; "path inclines" or "path slopes" sufficient
5. "One taller / one lower" — said twice across paragraphs; keep second (under canopy looking up), revise or cut first

**Temporal register:** Untended, human-empty, "outside of time." Grass cut but not recently. Acorns and old samaras coexist on ground. Seasonal rules slightly suspended.

### GROUP 1: THE COMMONS

Campus-like open lawn. Familiar, legible landscape. The walker is comfortable.

### 1. OAK — Quercus alba (White Oak)

**Principle:** Labor and Capital — the cyclical transformation between writing labor and textual capital.

**Terrain:** Open mown lawn, gravel path. High point of the walk. Full sky exposure.<br/>
**Enclosure:** None — solitary specimen in open space.<br/>
**Body:** Looking up and out. The tree's scale registers first.<br/>
**Path:** Gravel, level.

**Key visual:** Massive lone tree. Broad horizontal branching, wide-spreading crown. Deeply furrowed gray bark. Acorns scattered on lawn.

**Vocabulary:** _bole_ (1st use — the massive trunk), _crown_. Light density. Establishing the register contract with the reader.

**Central image:** Massiveness accrued slowly. The tree that holds value over centuries. Load-bearing.

**Botanical notes:** 60–100+ ft. Spirally arranged lobed leaves. Bark deeply furrowed, gray to dark brown. Acorn mast feeds entire ecosystems. Can reach 800–900 years. The keystone species.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Acorn-mast economy feeding entire communities. Labor accumulated into standing capital over centuries.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking.

---

### 2. ELM — Ulmus americana (American Elm) + Princeton cultivar + Ulmus parvifolia (Lacebark)

**Principle:** Six Moves / Operational Vocabulary — naming operations writers perform on information.

**Terrain:** Gentle descent from oak's lawn. Canopy corridor formed by two arching elms.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Corridor — allée remnant. Architecture that announces its own incompleteness.<br/>
**Body:** Looking up through arch. Walking through shaped space.<br/>
**Path:** Gravel, slight descent.

**Key visual:** Mismatched pair — one American elm (tall, vase-shaped), one Princeton cultivar (shorter, younger, same form but ten years in the ground at most). The asymmetry _is_ the DED story, unnamed. Lacebark elm (mottled exfoliating bark) stands off to one side as taxonomic range.

**Vocabulary:** _cultivar_, _exfoliating_ (lacebark bark), _allée_. Light density. Species-naming license established here: "a Princeton cultivar, shorter, ten years in the ground at most."

**Central image:** Incomplete corridor. The vocabulary as canopy architecture — when the framing vocabulary dies, the structure collapses, and what it made possible becomes visible only in absence. The asymmetric leaf base: things that look balanced aren't.

**Botanical notes:** American elm: iconic vase silhouette, trunk divides into arching branches, dark gray bark with flat braided ridges. Leaves doubly serrate, asymmetric at base. Princeton: DED-resistant hybrid, similar form but shorter/younger. Lacebark: mottled exfoliating bark, distinct visual texture.

**Double-voiced resonance:** DED as what happens when framing vocabulary dies. The allée remnant as incomplete infrastructure — gaps where elms were.

**Decisions made:** DED told through asymmetry, not naming. Princeton cultivar as replacement. Three elms, three visual signatures.

**Open questions:**
- Lacebark placement: off to side (specimen) or in row (another replacement)? Recommendation: off to side.

---

### 3. MAPLE — Acer saccharum (Sugar Maple) + Acer rubrum (Red Maple)

**Principle:** Economy and Technology in Composition — writing as technology, substituting capital for labor.

**Terrain:** Slight dip in ground. First time walker is _among_ trees rather than approaching one.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Cluster. Multiple trees surrounding the path. Progressive enclosure: solitary (oak) → corridor (elm) → cluster (maple).<br/>
**Body:** Looking around at multiple trees, then looking down (samaras on ground).<br/>
**Path:** Gravel, continuing descent.

**Key visual:** Small group of taller sugar maples and smaller planted red maples. Samaras scattered on ground — helicoptering seeds everyone has held.

**Vocabulary:** _samara_. One strong term. Contextually clear from bodily memory; the word names what the reader already knows.

**Central image:** Sap as invisible infrastructure — the tree's economy runs on a pipeline you can't see until you tap it. Samaras as distribution technology: seeds carrying accumulated genetic capital into new territory.

**Botanical notes:** Sugar maple: 35+ m, palmate leaf, deeply furrowed bark. Red maple: smaller, more adaptable, colonizes disturbed sites. Samaras: paired winged fruits that spin in dispersal. Sap flows on freeze-thaw cycle.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Invisible infrastructure made visible by tapping. Technology built into the body. The maple's samara is an evolved technology for distribution.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking. Spatial role clarified but drafting not yet prioritized.

### TRANSITION 1: Commons → Toxic Garden

**Vocabulary:** _ecotone_ (the boundary between communities), _aspect_ (compass orientation of a slope — south-facing creates warmth). Two terms deployed at the shift point; the vocabulary itself signals you've entered new territory.

**Terrain:** Path narrows. The familiar lawn ends. Ground shifts from open grass to something more enclosed. South-facing shelf creates a microclimate pocket justifying Mediterranean/subtropical species.

**Path:** Gravel transitions to flagstone. Surface change the body registers.

**Candidate formulation:** "The ecotone arrives as a change in the path."

**Spatial logic:** The land itself creates enclosure — south-facing shelf, narrowing path, dense planting. No literal wall needed (walled garden concept reserved, not rejected).

**Open questions:**
- Stone wall: reserved from this transition (Adventure closing already has "brick building"; wall may be needed later). Low stone wall may appear here or only at closing frame.

### GROUP 2: THE TOXIC GARDEN

South-facing walled section (walled by terrain, not masonry). Microclimate pocket. All three species are deadly — unannounced. Enclosed, shadowed, slightly ominous. Moss and leaf litter.

### 4. OLEANDER — Nerium oleander

**Principle:** Textual Accumulation — archives give analytics; accumulated text becomes analyzable capital.

**Terrain:** South-facing shelf. Warm, sheltered. Dense planting.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Surrounded by lush growth. Walled by terrain.<br/>
**Body:** Surrounded. The plants are showy, attractive. No visible threat.<br/>
**Path:** Flagstone.

**Key visual:** Mass planting — proliferated, lush, showy. Spectacular flower clusters in warm colors. The beauty is the point; the toxicity is unmarked.

**Vocabulary:** _(habit)_ — growth form — or near-zero. **Deliberately vocabulary-sparse.** The absence of technical language mirrors the plant's deception: it doesn't present as something that needs caution.

**Central image:** Beauty and danger in accumulation. Propagates from cuttings (one text spawns many). Toxic: accumulated material is potent, not inert. The archive is not safe; it's _powerful_.

**Botanical notes:** 6–7 m, multi-stemmed evergreen. Long narrow leathery leaves in whorls of three. Terminal flower clusters, 3–4 inch blooms. **All parts highly toxic** — cardiac glycosides lethal to humans and livestock. Propagates effortlessly from cuttings.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Proliferates from clones. Evergreen persistence — material doesn't decay. The archive that looks beautiful and is lethal.

**Candidate formulation:** "All three are deadly. None of them announce this."

**Open questions:**
- Near-zero vocabulary density: is the absence too clever, or does oleander need at least one term to maintain the register contract? Current recommendation: lean into the silence.

---

### 5. YEW — Taxus baccata (English Yew) or T. brevifolia (Pacific Yew)

**Principle:** YOLO Up, Constrain Down — maximizing early possibility before introducing constraint.

**Terrain:** Shelf continues. Dark, still space.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Enclosed, dark. Only 2–3 specimens but they dominate through presence.<br/>
**Body:** Looking at and into — the hollow trunk invites close inspection.<br/>
**Path:** Flagstone, moss between stones.

**Key visual:** Ancient, hollow-trunked specimens. Reddish-brown scaly bark flaking to reveal purple underbark. Dark flat needles. Fleshy red arils (the only non-toxic part). The heartwood is gone; the outer living wood continues.

**Vocabulary:** _heartwood_, _cambium_ (1st use — survival), _aril_. Denser. Language gets strange with the space.

**Central image:** "The heartwood is gone. What remains is a shell of living cambium around nothing." The hollow trunk that remains alive — generation continues even after the center is open. Sets up cambium call-and-response with hemlock.

**Botanical notes:** Evergreen conifer. Extremely slow-growing. Among the oldest living organisms — 2,000–5,000+ years. Ancient specimens dramatically hollow. Wood strong and elastic (longbow material). Taxine alkaloids lethal in all parts except arils. Taxol (chemotherapy drug) from Pacific yew bark.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Possibility persisting across millennia. Longbow wood — unconstrained force given shape. Deadly and life-saving from the same organism. Keeps generating after appearing to have nothing left inside.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking.

### 6. HOLLY — Ilex opaca (American Holly)

**Principle:** Infrastructure Visibility / Difficulty as Diagnostic — difficulty as signal exposing invisible constraints.

**Terrain:** Understory. Dense shade beneath taller trees.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Dense. The holly grows beneath and between. Dark.<br/>
**Body:** Looking down for the first time. The berries and spiny leaves demand close attention to what's underfoot and at hand-height.<br/>
**Path:** Flagstone, leaf litter accumulating.

**Key visual:** Dark glossy evergreen foliage with spiny margins. Bright red drupes (berries) — conspicuous precisely when everything else has dropped its leaves. The infrastructure that becomes visible upon breakdown.

**Vocabulary:** _understory_, _drupe_, _(heterophylly)_ — the condition of having different leaf forms on the same plant (spiny below, smooth above). Moderate density.

**Central image:** Invisible for most of the year — becomes conspicuous only when everything around it has broken down. Spines concentrated where contact occurs: difficulty as signal at points of friction.

**Botanical notes:** Evergreen, 40–50 ft. Thick leathery glossy leaves with spiny margins — spines concentrated on lower, reachable leaves; higher leaves smooth (heterophylly as constraint responding to threat). Dioecious. Red drupes persist through winter. Hardiest broadleaf evergreen. Shade-tolerant understory tree.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Infrastructure visible upon breakdown. Spines where friction occurs. The diagnostic appears where contact happens.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking.

### TRANSITION 2: Toxic Garden → Wet Forest

**Vocabulary:** _(canopy closure)_. One term. Felt overhead — the canopy closes above the walker before the wet forest begins.

**Terrain:** Descent toward water. The south-facing shelf drops. Light changes — filtered. The stone wall happens here.

**Path:** Flagstone transitions to boardwalk. The shift to boardwalk signals wet ground — the walker is being carried over terrain that can't support direct walking.

**Spatial logic:** The canopy closure is registered bodily (light dimming, air cooling) before it's named. The transition is environmental before it's lexical.

### GROUP 3: THE WET FOREST

Path opens onto water. Boardwalk over wet ground. The walker is INSIDE a system, not viewing specimens. Most immersive section. Silence. Deep duff. Filtered green light.

### 7. CEDAR — Thuja occidentalis (Northern White-Cedar / Arborvitae)

**Principle:** Permission Flags — writers develop agency by assessing variable constraint properties.

**Terrain:** Stream-level. Wet ground. The cedar is planted as hedge-like screen.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Threshold. The walker passes _through_ the cedar screen into the wet forest.<br/>
**Body:** Passing through. Aromatic — the scent registers as you brush past or the air changes.<br/>
**Path:** Boardwalk.

**Key visual:** Dense pyramidal form planted as hedge/screen. Reddish-brown fibrous bark peeling in strips. Flattened fan-like sprays of scale-like foliage. Aromatic when disturbed. The living wall. The tree whose primary cultural function is regulating what passes through.

**Vocabulary:** _spray/frond_ (foliage form), _fibrous_ (bark texture), _(volatile)_ (aromatic compounds). Moderate density. Environmental vocabulary begins — words shift from structural (heartwood, drupe) to atmospheric (volatile, fibrous).

**Central image:** The hedge tree. Controls access, defines boundaries. Cedar chests preserve by constraining what enters. "Tree of life" that functions as a wall. Permission flags made botanical.

**Botanical notes:** Evergreen conifer, 40–80 ft. Narrow conical form, dense. Bark fibrous, peeling in vertical strips. Foliage in flattened fan-like sprays, aromatic. Wood rot-resistant — prized for contact with water and soil. Most commonly encountered as hedge material. Name "arborvitae" from Cartier's scurvy cure.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Controls access, defines boundaries. Rot-resistant: constraints that endure. The threshold one passes through.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking.

### 8. ALDER — Alnus rubra (Red Alder) or A. glutinosa (Black Alder)

**Principle:** Constraint Sequencing — temporal sequence of constraint arrival shapes outcomes more than constraint content.

**Terrain:** Stream bank. Wet ground. The alder grows where water meets land.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Open to water on one side. The stream is now audible and visible.<br/>
**Body:** Looking across — the stream, the opposite bank. The pale bark catches light.<br/>
**Path:** Boardwalk.

**Key visual:** Pale mottled bark — ashy-gray-white, often colonized by white lichen. The most visually distinctive bark in the walk. Tiny persistent cone-like strobiles (the signature identification feature). Pioneer species.

**Vocabulary:** _strobiles_, _clade_, _pioneer_, _lenticels_. **Densest passage** — the vocabulary peak. The alder is where the reader encounters the most technical language.

**Central image:** The pioneer — arrives first, changes the ground for everything that follows. Nitrogen-fixing: transforms the soil's chemistry, enabling the forest that replaces it. "The sequence is the design." Short-lived but transformative.

**Botanical notes:** Deciduous, 5–30 m. Thin smooth bark, ashy-gray-white, mottled. Ovate leaves with blunt serrations. Small persistent strobiles (cone-like fruiting bodies, ~1 inch) that persist through winter. Fixes atmospheric nitrogen through Frankia root nodules — grows in nutrient-poor soil, enriches it for later species. Red alder: bright rusty-red color when bark bruised/scraped. Wood soft but water-resistant — hardens when submerged.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Arrives first, changes ground for everything after. The sequence is the design. Enables the forest that replaces it. Transforms the chemical environment.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking.

---

### 9. HEMLOCK — Tsuga canadensis (Eastern Hemlock) or T. heterophylla (Western Hemlock)

**Principle:** Citation as Dependency — citations declare dependencies; trace value appropriation.

**Terrain:** Deep shade. Canopy fully closed. Needle duff ankle-deep. Quiet. Most immersive point.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Full canopy closure. The walker is enclosed above and below (duff, canopy).<br/>
**Body:** Looking up into canopy (the drooping leader, the feathery texture), then attention snaps to girdling scar on trunk.<br/>
**Path:** Boardwalk over needle duff.

**Key visual:** Feathery dark canopy with slender drooping leader. Deep green filtered light. Then: the girdling scar — a deliberate ring cut through the bark, the visible evidence that someone severed the cambium. The violence in the peaceful space.

**Vocabulary:** _duff_ (decomposed needle layer), _girdling_ (the wound — cambium's 2nd use, as destruction), _(climax)_ (climax forest species). Moderate density. Environmental + the wound.

**Central image:** The girdling scar. Evidence of the last management decision before the arboretum went untended. The violence arrives in the most peaceful space, not the ominous one. Cambium call-and-response with yew: two fates for the same tissue.

**Botanical notes:** Evergreen conifer, 30–60 m. Narrow pyramidal crown, semi-pendulous branches, distinctive drooping leader (curved topmost shoot). Bark deeply furrowed on old trees. Needles short, flat, dark green above, two silvery stomatal bands below. Fine feathery texture. Most shade-tolerant conifer. Currently devastated by hemlock woolly adelgid (Adelges tsugae). 500+ year lifespan.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Grows dependent on canopy structure others created. Woolly adelgid as dependency network failing: organism from another system destroys what was never designed to resist it. Citation infrastructure visible when it fails. Persistence in shade — texts waiting in someone else's canopy.

**Girdling rationale:** Land managers girdle heavily adelgid-infested hemlocks to prevent spread — a triage decision. In an untended arboretum, the girdled hemlock is evidence of the last human decision. Girdling severs the dependency network; the tree may still stand but connections that made it alive are broken.

**Candidate formulation:** "The duff is ankle-deep."

**Open questions:**
- How much adelgid story to include? Girdling is a human act, not a disease symptom, so the text may need to gesture at _why_ someone did this — but naming the adelgid would parallel the elm's unnamed DED in reverse. Decision: medium urgency, not blocking.

### GROUP 4: THE RAVINE

Boardwalk gone. Ground tilts. Stream audible below. Topography creates microclimates solving the impossible-combination problem: dry rim, sheltered slope, saturated bottom. Walk descends to lowest point. Closing frame happens at the bottom.

### 10. STAR ANISE — Illicium floridanum (Florida Anise) or I. verum (Chinese Star Anise)

**Principle:** Circulation — circulation shapes writing at every stage, not only after a text is finished.

**Terrain:** Sheltered moist slope. Mid-ravine.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Sheltered but not enclosed — the slope provides cover, the canopy is opening.<br/>
**Body:** Smelling before seeing. Fragrance arrives first. The approach is sensory before visual.<br/>
**Path:** Bare earth.

**Key visual:** Multi-stemmed evergreen shrub, glossy dark leaves. Star-shaped nodding flowers. The signature eight-pointed star fruit (aggregate of follicles). Fragrance is the primary encounter — anise/licorice scent from crushed leaves.

**Vocabulary:** _follicle_ (the individual carpel of the star fruit), _volatile_ (aromatic compounds — echoes cedar). Moderate density.

**Central image:** Fragrance circulates before the substance arrives — "seductive" (the Latin name _illicium_ = allurement). The culinary/toxic distinction: same botanical structure circulated through different pathways produces opposite effects. Value depends on the route.

**Botanical notes:** Evergreen shrub to small tree, 3–7 m. Glossy elliptic leaves, aromatic when crushed. Star-shaped flowers with 20–30 strap-shaped petals. Aggregate fruit in eight-pointed star. Only I. verum is culinary; all other species toxic. Name from Latin _illicium_ = seductive/allurement.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Value radiating outward in a star pattern — circulation as geometry. What circulates before the substance arrives. Same material through different pathways = opposite effects.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking.

---

### 11. OLIVE — Olea europaea

**Principle:** Appropriation and Value — value appropriated at each stage of circulation, not only through extraction.

**Terrain:** Dry sun-exposed rim of ravine. The microclimate pocket: warm, rocky, well-drained.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Exposed. Sun reaches here. Open to sky again but from lower elevation.<br/>
**Body:** Looking at the gnarled trunk — the contorted, sculptural form demands close looking.<br/>
**Path:** Bare earth/stone.

**Key visual:** Gnarled, twisted, deeply fissured trunk — ancient olives look wrung by centuries. Silvery-green canopy shimmering in light (leaves dark gray-green above, silvery-white below). Small scale — 8–15 m.

**Vocabulary:** _coppice_ (regrowth from cut stump), _rootstock_ (wild root base for grafting), _graft union_ (the join between rootstock and cultivar). Moderate-dense. All about joining/grafting.

**Central image:** Grafting as appropriation technology — one organism's root system supporting another's production. Raw olives inedible (intensely bitter) — value requires processing, transformation through labor. The tree that regenerates from its roots: appropriation doesn't destroy the source.

**Botanical notes:** Evergreen, 8–15 m. Gnarled, twisted trunk, deeply fissured and hollow with age. Small narrow lance-shaped leaves, silvery below. Fruit is a drupe, inedible raw (oleuropein). Propagates by grafting: wild rootstock + cultivated variety. Regenerates from root when cut. Can live 1,000–2,000+ years. One of the oldest cultivated trees (~7,000 years).

**Double-voiced resonance:** Olive press economics — who owns tree vs. who harvests vs. who presses vs. who trades. Grafting as joining two organisms. Bitter until processed: value requires labor. Ancient, gnarled, and still productive.

**Open questions:** None currently blocking.

---

### 12. TUPELO — Nyssa sylvatica (Black Tupelo / Black Gum)

**Principle:** Corpse, Compost, Garden — textual decomposition enables future growth more than preservation.

**Terrain:** Saturated bottom of ravine. Standing water. The lowest point.<br/>
**Enclosure:** Bottom. Open to sky above but surrounded by ravine walls.<br/>
**Body:** Looking up from below. The walker is in the pot.<br/>
**Path:** Saturated ground. Feet wet.

**Key visual:** Straight tapering trunk with horizontal branches at right angles — layered, tiered, unlike any other tree. Alligator-hide bark — deeply furrowed, thick, blocky ridges. Fiery fall color: scarlet, crimson, orange, purple. Standing in water at the base.

**Vocabulary:** _senescence_ (the process of aging/dying that produces the fall color), _bole_ (3rd use — alligator-hide), _buttress_ (flared trunk base in wet ground). Culminating density. Terms carry accumulated force — the reader has been building vocabulary for 11 passages.

**Central image:** "Senescence here is not decline but display." The most fiery fall color — spectacular transformation through dying. Alligator-hide bark as surface record of sustained transformation. Cross-grained wood that resists splitting: decomposed but not destroyed. Named for a water nymph; rooted in swamp.

**Botanical notes:** Deciduous, 60–80+ ft. Straight tapering trunk, horizontal branches at 90°, tiered profile. Bark "alligator hide" — deeply furrowed, blocky ridges. Leaves glossy dark green, clustered at branch tips. Fall color claimed as most brilliant of all deciduous trees. Blue drupes for migrating songbirds. Cross-grained wood — nearly impossible to split. Deep sensitive taproot. Present in 35+ forest cover types, dominant in none. Name "tupelo" from Creek: _ito_ (tree) + _opilwa_ (swamp).

**Double-voiced resonance:** Present everywhere, dominant nowhere — the generalist's composted fragments across 35 cover types. The most spectacular dying. Trunk becomes bee gum: dead hollow housing living industry. Cross-grained: resists splitting even in decomposition.

## Appendix: Design Principles for Drafting

1. **Second-person address throughout.** Triple-coded: naturalist guide / IF parser / pedagogical address. All three registers active; none named.
2. **Backward references enacted, not reported.** No parser-style state reports ("The large oak is now to your south"). The oak stays present through contrast — diminishing openness, arriving shade.
3. **Species-naming license.** The narrator reads trees as the opening frame promised: "labeled in a language you may or may not already read." Binomials in body text; genus-only for nexus page names.
4. **Temporal register: "outside of time."** Untended, not abandoned. Seasonal rules slightly suspended. Things fall and stay.
5. **~1 specialized term per passage, sometimes 2–3, some deliberately bare.** Total across 12 trees + transitions: ~20–25 terms. Enough to flavor the register without becoming a glossary.
6. **The vocabulary IS the enclosure arc.** Sparse at the open lawn; dense at the alder; culminating at the tupelo. The lexicon and the landscape do the same thing.
7. **Girdling used exactly once.** Its force depends on singularity.
8. **All outlinks are unlabeled.** IF references (playable Zork, Adventure source, "Get Lamp" documentary), botanical references — linked without explanation. Only readers who catch the echoes catch the layer.
