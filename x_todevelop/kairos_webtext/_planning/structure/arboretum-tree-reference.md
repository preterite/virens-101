# Arboretum Tree Reference: Botanical Details for Drafting

Each entry provides: habitat/environment, physical appearance, and the conceptual resonance (from trees_principles.md) to calibrate the "double-voiced" register.

---

## 1. Oak (Quercus) — Labor and Capital

**Concept:** The cyclical transformation between writing labor and textual capital constitutes an actual economic cycle.

**Habitat/Environment:** Oaks are keystone species across the Northern Hemisphere, thriving in an extraordinary range of habitats — Mediterranean semi-desert to subtropical rainforest, dry upland ridges to rich bottomlands. White oaks favor mesic to dry woods, warm southwest-facing slopes, rocky hillsides. Live oaks hug the coastal plain. They are components of hardwood forests worldwide and support more lepidopteran species than any other tree genus — over 950 species of caterpillars, feeding entire ecosystems.

**Appearance:** Massive, long-lived deciduous or evergreen trees (depending on species), 60–100+ feet tall. Characteristically wide-spreading horizontal branches forming a broad, rounded crown when open-grown; in forests, a long straight trunk with a narrower canopy. Deeply lobed leaves arranged spirally, dark green above, lighter below. Bark deeply furrowed, gray to dark brown. Fruit is the acorn — a nut seated in a scaly cup — which matures in one to two seasons and provides mast for dozens of species. Wood extremely hard, durable, used for barrels (holding value), shipbuilding, furniture. Oaks can reach 800–900 years old with trunk diameters of 3–4 meters.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Massiveness accrued slowly. Acorn-mast economy feeding entire communities. The tree that holds value over centuries. Load-bearing.

## 2. Elm (Ulmus) — Six Moves / Operational Vocabulary

**Concept:** Naming the operations writers perform on information, in addition to the temporal stages writers move through, makes those operations distinguishable, specifiable, and available for deliberate sequencing.

**Habitat/Environment:** Elms inhabit moist to mesic deciduous woodlands, floodplains, stream banks, savannas, and woodland borders. The American elm (U. americana) thrives in rich bottomlands and swampy ground but also on hillsides and uplands — remarkably tolerant of a wide range of soils, pH levels, and urban conditions. Extremely hardy, surviving temperatures to −40°C. Once the most ubiquitous street tree in American cities — entire streetscapes defined by their canopy architecture.

**Appearance:** Medium to large deciduous trees, 60–100+ feet, with the iconic vase-shaped silhouette: the trunk divides into several leading branches at sharp angles that arc upward and outward, then divide into long, flexible branchlets whose tips float lightly, creating a broad, rounded canopy. On narrower streets, elms planted on both sides arch together into a leafy tunnel — a "cathedral-like ceiling." Bark dark gray with flat braided ridges. Leaves ovate-elliptic, doubly serrate, distinctively asymmetric at the base (one side lower than the other). Fruit a small wafer-like samara — a single seed surrounded by a flat, oval papery wing, dispersed by wind in enormous quantities (50,000 per pound). Devastated by Dutch elm disease (a fungus introduced from Asia, spread by bark beetles) — millions killed since the 1920s.

**Double-voiced resonance:** The tree that _shaped_ the streetscape — vocabulary as canopy architecture. Dutch elm disease as what happens when the framing vocabulary dies: the structure collapses, and what it made possible becomes visible only in its absence. The asymmetric leaf base — things that look balanced aren't.

## 3. Maple (Acer) — Economy and Technology in Composition

**Concept:** Technology substitutes capital for labor. Writing is a technology in precisely this sense. Composition studies is ground for economic inquiry.

**Habitat/Environment:** Approximately 132 species across East Asia, Europe, North Africa, and North America — one of the most common genera of trees in the Northern Hemisphere. Maples favor moist, organically rich, slightly acidic, well-drained soils. Many species are understory trees in shady woodlands (Japanese maple grows beneath taller canopy). North American sugar maples (A. saccharum) dominate mesic forests of the Northeast and upper Midwest, creating the spectacular autumn displays. Red maples colonize disturbed sites, wetlands, and uplands alike — among the most adaptable of all North American trees.

**Appearance:** Deciduous trees and shrubs ranging from 6 meters (Japanese maple) to 35+ meters (sugar maple). Most species bear the immediately recognizable palmate leaf — hand-shaped, with 3–7 pointed lobes. Bark varies: smooth and gray-green on young trees, becoming furrowed and plated with age. All maples produce distinctive winged fruits called samaras — paired "helicopter" seeds joined at the base, each seed bearing a single papery wing that spins it through the air during dispersal. The samara's wing is an evolved technology for distribution. Sugar maple sap flows in late winter when freezing nights and warm days create pressure differentials — harvested by tapping the trunk, an extraction of the tree's internal economy that runs on an invisible pipeline.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Sap as invisible infrastructure — the tree's economy runs on a pipeline you can't see until you tap it. Samaras as distribution technology — seeds designed for dispersal, each carrying accumulated genetic capital into new territory. The maple's technology is built into its body.

## 4. Oleander (Nerium) — Textual Accumulation

**Concept:** Textual accumulation transforms discrete performances into analyzable capital available for future reconfiguration. Archives give analytics.

**Habitat/Environment:** Oleander is native to the Mediterranean, North Africa, and Southeast Asia — a plant of dry streambeds (wadis), rocky slopes, and seasonally arid watercourses. Naturalized across warm-temperate and subtropical zones worldwide. Extremely drought-tolerant once established, though responds vigorously to moisture. Thrives in full sun, tolerates poor soils, salt spray, and urban pollution. Often the last shrub standing in neglected landscapes — persistent, invasive in some regions, colonizing roadsides and disturbed ground.

**Appearance:** A rapidly growing, rounded, broadleaf evergreen shrub or small tree, reaching 6–7 meters, often multi-stemmed. Leaves are long, narrow, leathery, lance-shaped (resembling olive leaves — the name Nerium derives from the Greek for olive), dark green, arranged in whorls of three. Flowers are spectacular: terminal clusters of 3–4 inch blooms in red, pink, salmon, yellow, or white, appearing profusely in warm months. Fruit is a long, narrow pod splitting to release tufted seeds for wind dispersal. **All parts of the plant are highly toxic** — one of the most poisonous commonly cultivated plants, containing cardiac glycosides lethal to humans and livestock. Propagates effortlessly from cuttings — a single plant can generate an unlimited archive of clones.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Generates prolifically — beauty and danger in accumulation. Propagates from cuttings (one text spawns many). Toxic: accumulated material is potent, not inert. The archive is not safe; it's _powerful_. Evergreen persistence — material doesn't decay, it remains available.

## 5. Yew (Taxus) — YOLO Up, Constrain Down

**Concept:** Maximizing early possibility before introducing constraint creates conditions for accumulation that cannot be achieved by frontloading requirements.

**Habitat/Environment:** Yews are distributed across temperate regions of the Northern Hemisphere — Europe, Asia, North America. The English yew (T. baccata) favors chalky, limestone soils and is famously associated with churchyards (some specimens predate the churches beside them). Pacific yew (T. brevifolia) grows as an understory tree in old-growth forests of the Pacific Northwest. Shade-tolerant — among the most shade-tolerant of all conifers — capable of surviving beneath dense canopy for decades. Thrives in cool, moist conditions. Hardy and extremely long-lived.

**Appearance:** Evergreen conifers, varying from shrubs to trees reaching 10–20 meters (some ancient specimens larger). Bark reddish-brown, thin, scaly, flaking to reveal reddish-purple underbark. Needles are flat, dark green above, lighter below, spirally arranged but often appearing in two flat rows. Grows very slowly. **Among the oldest living organisms on Earth** — individual yews have been estimated at 2,000–5,000+ years; the Fortingall Yew in Scotland may be 3,000–9,000 years old. Ancient yews develop dramatically hollow trunks — the heartwood decays while the outer living wood continues to grow, creating vast internal cavities. New trunks can grow inside the shell of the old one. The wood is exceptionally strong and elastic — the preferred material for English longbows. Produces fleshy red arils (not true berries) surrounding a single seed; the aril is the only non-toxic part — all other parts contain taxine alkaloids, which are lethal. Taxol, derived from Pacific yew bark, is an important chemotherapy drug.

**Double-voiced resonance:** The hollow trunk that remains alive — generation continues even when the center is open. The oldest living things: possibility persisting across millennia. Longbow wood — the flex and snap of unconstrained force given shape. Deadly and life-saving from the same organism (taxol). A tree that keeps generating even after it appears to have nothing left inside.

## 6. Holly (Ilex) — Infrastructure Visibility / Difficulty as Diagnostic

**Concept:** Difficulty functions as diagnostic signal, exposing infrastructural constraints invisible until a writer pushes against them.

**Habitat/Environment:** Hollies are remarkably widespread — roughly 480 species across temperate and subtropical regions worldwide. American holly (I. opaca) ranges from coastal Massachusetts to central Florida and eastern Texas, found in rich bottomlands, swamps, upland pine sites, and hammocks. Grows best on moist, slightly acid, well-drained soils, but survives on an astonishing variety from sterile coastal sand to thin mountain soils. **The hardiest known broadleaf evergreen tree** — surviving temperatures to −29°C. Shade-tolerant: most often an understory tree beneath taller pines and hardwoods.

**Appearance:** Evergreen trees or shrubs (American holly typically 40–50 feet, sometimes more). Dark, dense foliage — leaves are thick, leathery, glossy dark green above with spiny-toothed margins (the famous prickly leaves). The spines are a defense mechanism — concentrated on lower, reachable leaves; higher leaves are often smooth-edged (the tree constrains differently depending on what threatens it). **Dioecious** — separate male and female trees; only females produce the bright red berries (drupes) that are the tree's most conspicuous feature, ripening in fall and persisting through winter. The berries become visible precisely when everything else has dropped its leaves — holly's infrastructure appears only when the surrounding system goes bare. Bark is smooth, light gray. Growth is slow.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Invisible for most of the year — a dark understory presence that becomes conspicuous only when everything around it has broken down. Spines concentrated where contact occurs (difficulty as signal at points of friction). Infrastructure visible upon breakdown.

## 7. Cedar / Arborvitae (Thuja) — Permission Flags

**Concept:** Writers develop agency by learning to assess the variable properties of diverse constraints and focus action upon the intervening differences.

**Habitat/Environment:** Northern white-cedar (T. occidentalis, also called arborvitae — "tree of life") grows in the humid eastern half of North America from the Gulf of St. Lawrence to southeastern Manitoba, south through the Lake States. Favors moist ground: swamps, stream banks, shores, and cool moist slopes. Also found on limestone cliffs and upland sites with adequate moisture. Annual precipitation 710–1170mm. Tolerates shade well, forming dense understory. The name "arborvitae" dates from the 16th century when the French explorer Jacques Cartier learned from Indigenous peoples to use the vitamin-C-rich foliage to treat scurvy.

**Appearance:** Evergreen conifer, 40–80 feet tall, with a narrow, conical, often buttressed trunk and dense, pyramidal crown. Bark reddish-brown, fibrous, peeling in narrow vertical strips. Foliage is distinctive: flattened, fan-like sprays of tiny scale-like leaves (not needles), aromatic when crushed. The wood is exceptionally rot-resistant and termite-resistant — prized for products in contact with water and soil (posts, shingles, canoes, chests). **Most commonly encountered as hedge material** — planted to create dense screens, boundaries, privacy barriers, windbreaks. The living wall. When pruned, grows even denser. Widely used in garden design to **control access, define boundaries, and create enclosures** — a tree whose primary cultural function is regulating what passes through.

**Double-voiced resonance:** The hedge tree — controls access, defines boundaries. Cedar chests preserve by constraining what enters. Rot-resistant wood: constraints that endure. "Tree of life" that functions as a wall. Permission flags made botanical.

## 8. Alder (Alnus) — Constraint Sequencing

**Concept:** The temporal sequence in which constraints arrive shapes learning outcomes more decisively than the content of those constraints.

**Habitat/Environment:** Alders are found near streams, rivers, ponds, lakes, marshes, and wetlands across the Northern Hemisphere and parts of western South America, from sea level to 2,500 meters. Red alder (A. rubra) ranges from Southeast Alaska to central California, nearly always within 200 km of the Pacific coast. Black alder (A. glutinosa) is widespread through Eurasia. Alders thrive in damp, cool areas — but here's the key: **they are pioneer species, among the first trees to colonize disturbed or bare ground.** They rapidly cover former burns, clearcuts, and landslides, often preventing other species from establishing until alder has changed the soil. This is because **alders fix atmospheric nitrogen** through symbiotic bacteria (Frankia alni) in their root nodules — they can grow in nutrient-poor soils where few other trees thrive, and they _enrich the soil_ for everything that follows.

**Appearance:** Deciduous trees and large shrubs, ranging from 5-meter shrubs to 30-meter trees (red alder is the largest, North America's biggest alder). Red alder bark is thin, smooth, ashy-gray-white, mottled, often colonized by white lichen and moss — distinctive in Pacific Northwest forests. Leaves are ovate, 7–15 cm, with bluntly serrated edges. The most distinctive feature: **small, cone-like fruiting bodies called strobiles**, about an inch long, that persist on the tree through winter — looking like tiny pinecones on a deciduous tree. Male catkins are long and dangling; female catkins shorter, woody, persistent. Red alder's name comes from the bright rusty-red color of bruised or scraped bark. The wood is relatively soft — not valued as structural timber — but water-resistant, used for pilings in Venice and for sluice gates, water mills, musical instruments. Alder wood **does not rot when submerged** — it hardens in water.

**Double-voiced resonance:** The pioneer: arrives first, changes the ground for everything that follows. Nitrogen-fixing — transforms the soil's chemical environment, making it habitable for later arrivals. "The sequence is the design" — alder's arrival _enables_ the forest that replaces it. Short-lived (60–100 years) but transformative. The sequence is the design.

## 9. Hemlock (Tsuga) — Citation as Dependency

**Concept:** Citations declare the dependencies a text requires to succeed and trace the sequence through which value gets appropriated.

**Habitat/Environment:** Hemlocks are adapted to relatively moist, cool temperate areas with high rainfall, cool summers, and little water stress. Eastern hemlock (T. canadensis) ranges throughout the Great Lakes, Appalachian Mountains, and northeastern US/Maritime Canada — in rocky, cool, moist sites, ravines, north-facing slopes, and along streams. Western hemlock (T. heterophylla) dominates the Pacific Northwest from Sonoma County to the Kenai Peninsula. **Hemlocks are the most shade-tolerant conifers** — young plants grow beneath the canopy of other trees (Sitka spruce, Douglas-fir), persisting for decades in deep shade, waiting to exploit a gap in the canopy above. They eventually replace the less shade-tolerant trees in climax forest. Thin bark and shallow roots make them susceptible to fire and disturbance.

**Appearance:** Medium to large evergreen conifers, 30–60 meters tall, with a narrow, pyramidal crown, semi-pendulous branches, and a very slender, curved, drooping leader (the topmost shoot nods downward — a distinguishing silhouette). Bark red-brown and scaly on young trees, becoming deeply furrowed and gray-brown with age. Needles are short (1–2 cm), flat, dark green above with two silvery-white stomatal bands on the underside, attached by tiny stalks — the foliage gives a fine, feathery texture. Cones are small and numerous. Eastern hemlocks can live 500+ years (oldest recorded: 554 years). **Currently under devastating threat from the hemlock woolly adelgid** (Adelges tsugae), an introduced sap-sucking insect from East Asia — entire stands are dying across the southern Appalachians. Asian hemlocks are resistant to the pest; American hemlocks are not, having no evolutionary history with it.

**Double-voiced resonance:** The most shade-tolerant tree — grows _dependent_ on the canopy structure others created. The woolly adelgid as dependency networks breaking: an organism from another system destroys what was never designed to resist it. Citation networks are infrastructure that becomes visible when they fail. Persistence in shade — texts waiting for their moment in someone else's canopy.

## 10. Star Anise (Illicium) — Circulation

**Concept:** Circulation is not merely what happens after writing but a dynamic that shapes writing at every stage. Following Trimbur.

**Habitat/Environment:** Illicium species are native to Southeast Asia, the southeastern United States, Central America, and the Caribbean. Most are **understory plants** — evergreen to semi-evergreen shrubs and small trees growing in shade to part shade. In the US, Florida anise (I. floridanum) inhabits wet, swampy areas, wooded ravines, marshy stream margins along the Gulf coastal plain from Florida to Louisiana. Chinese star anise (I. verum) grows in the tropical-subtropical forests of southern China and Vietnam. All prefer moist, rich, acidic soils. The genus name comes from Latin _illicium_ — "seductive," "allurement" — referring to the plant's fragrance.

**Appearance:** Evergreen shrubs to small trees, typically 3–7 meters, multi-stemmed, rounded, bushy. Leaves are smooth, glossy, elliptic, dark olive-green, leathery, arranged alternately — **strongly aromatic when crushed** (anise/licorice scent). Flowers are unusual: nodding, star-shaped, with 20–30 narrow strap-shaped petals radiating outward in pink, red, yellow-green, or white. **The fruit is the signature feature: an aggregate of follicles arranged in a star-shaped whorl** — the iconic eight-pointed star of star anise. Each point of the star is a single carpel containing one glossy brown seed. Fruits split open at maturity. **Only I. verum (Chinese star anise) is culinary** — the spice used in five-spice powder, pho, chai. All other species are **toxic**. The distinction matters: circulation transforms the same botanical structure into either medicine or poison depending on the pathway from harvest to use.

**Double-voiced resonance:** The eight-pointed fruit — value radiating outward in a star pattern, circulation as geometry. Fragrance as what circulates before the substance arrives ("seductive" — the Latin name). The culinary/toxic distinction: the same material circulated through different pathways produces opposite effects. Value depends on the route.

## 11. Olive (Olea) — Appropriation and Value

**Concept:** Value gets appropriated at each stage of circulation — not just by capitalist extraction but through self-appropriation, communal use, and gift.

**Habitat/Environment:** The olive (O. europaea) is native to the Mediterranean basin — one of the oldest cultivated trees, with archaeological evidence of cultivation going back 6,000–7,000 years. Thrives in the Mediterranean climate: hot, dry summers and mild, wet winters. Tolerates poor, rocky, calcareous soils — grows on terraced hillsides across Greece, Italy, Spain, Turkey, North Africa. Remarkably drought-tolerant once established. Cannot survive prolonged hard frost. Now cultivated worldwide wherever Mediterranean climates occur (California, Australia, South Africa, Chile). **One of the defining plants of human civilization** — the olive branch as symbol of peace, the oil as economic foundation of ancient Mediterranean trade.

**Appearance:** A small to medium evergreen tree, typically 8–15 meters, with a short, squat, often gnarled and twisted trunk that becomes deeply fissured and hollow with age — ancient olives have enormous, sculptural, contorted trunks that look like they've been wrung by centuries. Crown is rounded, open, silvery. **Leaves are distinctive**: small, narrow, lance-shaped, 4–10 cm, leathery — dark gray-green above and **silvery-white below** (due to dense scales), giving the whole canopy a characteristic shimmering, silver-green appearance in the wind. Small white flowers appear in clusters. The fruit is the olive — a drupe (stone fruit), green becoming purple-black when fully ripe. Olives are **inedible raw** — intensely bitter due to oleuropein; they must be processed (brining, curing, pressing) to become food or oil. **Olives propagate readily by grafting** — a wild rootstock (oleaster) is grafted with a cultivated variety, combining the wild root's vigor with the cultivar's fruit quality. Ancient trees can be regenerated: cut to the ground, an olive regrows from the root. Individual trees may live 1,000–2,000+ years.

**Double-voiced resonance:** The economics of the olive press — who owns the tree vs. who harvests the fruit vs. who presses the oil vs. who trades it. Grafting as appropriation technology: one organism's root system supporting another's production. Raw olives are _bitter_ — value requires processing, transformation through labor. The tree that regenerates from its own roots: appropriation doesn't destroy the source. Ancient, gnarled, and still productive.

## 12. Tupelo (Nyssa) — Corpse, Compost, Garden

**Concept:** Textual decomposition enables future writing growth more effectively than preservation. The corpse of one text becomes compost for the next.

**Habitat/Environment:** Nyssa sylvatica (black tupelo, black gum, sour gum) is native to eastern North America from southern Ontario to central Florida and eastern Texas. A remarkable generalist — found in an extraordinary range of habitats from creek bottoms to dry upland ridges, swamp margins to mountain slopes at 900+ meters. Grows in 35+ forest cover types, never dominant in any of them but present in all. Tolerates both flooding and drought. The genus name **Nyssa refers to a Greek water nymph** — the mythological naming. The common name "tupelo" comes from Creek/Muscogee words: _ito_ (tree) + _opilwa_ (swamp). Tupelo honey (from the related water tupelo, N. aquatica) is a prized artisanal product.

**Appearance:** A medium to large deciduous tree, 60–80+ feet, with a distinctive silhouette: **a straight, tapering trunk rising continuously to the summit** with **horizontal branches extending at right angles** (90°), giving it a layered, tiered profile unlike any other tree. In forests, the crown is narrow and conical; open-grown, it spreads wider. Bark on old specimens becomes deeply furrowed with thick, blocky, irregular ridges — described as **"alligator hide"** — dark gray to reddish-brown. Leaves are simple, alternate, obovate, leathery, clustered at branch tips — glossy dark green in summer. **Fall color is the tupelo's most celebrated quality**: among the earliest trees to change, and widely claimed as **"the most fiery and brilliant"** of all deciduous trees — intense scarlet, crimson, orange, and purple, often appearing on individual leaves long before other trees begin to turn. Small dark blue drupes on female trees (dioecious) provide critical food for fall-migrating songbirds. The wood is extraordinarily cross-grained — nearly impossible to split — historically used for chopping blocks, tool handles, and bee gums (hollow trunk sections used as beehives). **Difficult to transplant** due to a deep, sensitive taproot.

**Double-voiced resonance:** Named for a water nymph; rooted in swamp. The most fiery fall color — spectacular transformation through dying. The tree that's present everywhere but dominant nowhere: the generalist's composted fragments, appearing across 35 cover types. Cross-grained wood that resists splitting: texts decomposed but not destroyed. Alligator-hide bark — the surface record of sustained transformation. The trunk that becomes a bee gum: a dead hollow that houses living industry.

## Closing Frame Reference: Van Helmont's Willow

**Jan Baptist van Helmont (1648, _Ortus Medicinae_, published posthumously):** Planted a 5-pound willow in 200 pounds of dried soil in a covered pot. Watered it for five years. The willow grew to 169 pounds; the soil lost only 2 ounces. He concluded (incorrectly) that the mass came entirely from water. The actual answer: most of a tree's dry mass is carbon fixed from atmospheric CO₂ via photosynthesis — the mass came from invisible sources (air and light), not the container (the soil, the pot). Van Helmont was wrong about the mechanism but right that the mass didn't come from the container.

**Richard Powers, _The Overstory_ (2018):** Retells this as the "bucket experiment." Specific passage to be located.

**For the arboretum closing:** The course is the pot. The writing is the tree. The mass came from somewhere the container cannot account for.
