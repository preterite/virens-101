# Fragment: Elbow + Arnold — Authenticity and the Sublimated Self

## Core

Elbow's pedagogy and Arnold's "The Buried Life" both point toward inaccessible interiority — not essence to be expressed but void to be reached toward. Reading Elbow through Arnold recovers him against social-turn critiques: the reaching matters, not the grasping.

## Elaboration

The social turn read Elbow as naive expressivist who believed in authentic selfhood available for direct transcription. Berlin's critique positioned expressivism as ideology — the writer's "authentic voice" as naturalized construct masking social determination. Bartholomae's "Writing with Teachers" sharpened the attack: Elbow's writing classroom was a fantasy of unmediated expression.

But Arnold's "The Buried Life" isn't about accessing essence. It's about the _inaccessibility_ of whatever's down there, glimpsed only in rare moments that immediately recede. The buried stream flows but can't be reached; what the poem performs is the act of reaching, not the act of grasping. If Elbow is read through Arnold, his position becomes deconstructively sophisticated rather than naive: you can't deconstruct an essence no one's claiming exists.

Close reading of Elbow's own texts supports this recovery. His hedges, qualifications, and metaphors consistently point toward something _approached_ rather than _captured_. "Voice" in Elbow isn't a property the writer possesses and transcribes; it's something that emerges through the act of writing, that writing _generates_ rather than _records_. The freewriting practice isn't "express what you feel" — it's "write past what you think you know and see what shows up." That's methodologically closer to Derrida's _différance_ than to Romantic expressivism.

The mask question follows naturally. If what's "inside" remains inaccessible, then every mode of self-presentation — academic, personal, observational, fictional — is a mask. Not mask as deception but mask as the necessary form through which reaching happens. The sublimated self isn't hidden _behind_ the mask; it's constituted _by_ the act of masking. Arnold knew this: the poem's speaker performs sincerity while acknowledging that the performance is all there is.

For the webtext's purposes, this recovery matters because it grants theoretical dignity to the personal and expressive registers the project employs — not as naive alternatives to scholarly argument but as additional masks through which the buried life gets reached toward. The arboretum's second-person observational voice, Grant's fictional interiority, Vera's designer perspective — all are modes of reaching, none claims to arrive.
