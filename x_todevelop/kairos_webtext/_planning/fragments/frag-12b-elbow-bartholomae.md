# Fragment: Elbow + Bartholomae — Four Voices and the Author Function

## Core

The Bartholomae-Elbow debate — writer vs. academic, personal voice vs. disciplinary convention — posed a binary the webtext refuses by performing four distinct voices simultaneously. "Edwards" operates as third-person author-function construct; the first person is reserved for the acknowledgments page, where its arrival becomes an event.

## Elaboration

Bartholomae's "Inventing the University" argued that students must appropriate the discourse of the academy — must learn to write _as_ academics before they can write _against_ academic convention with authority. Elbow's "Being a Writer vs. Being an Academic" countered that academic writing's conventions suppress the personal, experiential, exploratory modes that generate genuine thinking. The debate structured a generation of composition pedagogy as an either/or: teach students to write academically (Bartholomae) or teach students to write personally and let academic form follow (Elbow).

This webtext doesn't resolve the debate. It _occupies_ it — performing both positions simultaneously across four registers that the writer/academic binary can't contain:

**Grant's fiction** is Elbow's writer: voice emerging through experience, interiority made available through narrative, meaning generated in the act of writing rather than the act of citing. Grant discovers what he knows by articulating it. His track is expressivist pedagogy enacted as fiction.

**The nexus pages** are Bartholomae's academic: disciplinary convention, scholarly citation, theoretical argument, the discourse of composition studies addressed to composition scholars. The nexus pages speak _as_ the academy.

**The arboretum** is something neither Elbow nor Bartholomae theorized: second-person observational prose that is neither personal expression nor disciplinary argument. It's enacted attention — the reader's body moving through space, noticing, interpreting, without claiming either authentic selfhood or scholarly authority. The arboretum's voice belongs to no one and addresses everyone.

**Vera's design track** is the practitioner voice — neither personal nor academic but _professional_. The designer thinking through constraints, making decisions, explaining rationale. This voice exists in composition studies (in WPA literature, in pedagogy articles, in methods sections) but rarely gets theorized as a _mode_ distinct from both the personal and the scholarly.

The four voices together demonstrate that "writer vs. academic" was always an impoverished binary. Writing operates across multiple registers simultaneously; a single author can inhabit all of them within a single project. The question isn't which voice is authentic — all are masks (see [[frag-12a-elbow-arnold]]) — but which voice serves which purpose at which moment.

### "Edwards" as Third-Person Construct

The dev-log stubs and any meta-commentary about the webtext's design use "Edwards" or "the designer" or "the author" rather than "I." This isn't false modesty — it's a structural decision. Third-person reference treats authorship as a _function_ (in Foucault's sense) rather than an _identity_. The person who built this webtext is a role being performed, not a self being expressed.

The first person appears only on the acknowledgments page, where it arrives as a deliberate departure from the webtext's convention. The "I" that thanks collaborators, names influences, and addresses the reader directly gains force _because_ it hasn't been available elsewhere. The acknowledgments page is the one place where the masks come off — and even that is itself a mask, a convention of scholarly gratitude, which the reader who has followed the Elbow-Arnold thread will recognize.

### Implications for the Dev-Log Stubs

The 24 dev-log stubs should maintain this third-person convention. Where personal experience illustrates a design decision or theoretical claim, the stub can use general examples, composite cases, or third-person reference ("a designer facing this constraint might..."; "the decision to use index cards followed from..."). The stubs document design thinking without performing autobiography. Personal disclosure gets channeled through Vera's fiction (where it's transmuted into character) or reserved for the acknowledgments (where it's earned by the convention's rarity).

## Open

- How much of the Bartholomae-Elbow debate needs rehearsal for a Kairos audience? (They'll know it — brief framing, not history lesson)
- Does the four-voice taxonomy risk being too schematic? (The voices blur in practice — Grant's fiction draws on scholarly reading; the nexus pages use narrative examples; the arboretum's observation is informed by theoretical commitments)
- Where does the Foucault author-function reference land? (Brief gesture, not extended engagement — composition scholars know the concept)
- Connection to the webtext's own Bartholomae problem: the nexus pages "invent the university" in real time, performing exactly the disciplinary appropriation Bartholomae described, while the fiction tracks refuse it
- Does third-person convention need explicit justification on the webtext itself, or does the acknowledgments page's first-person arrival make the convention visible retroactively?
