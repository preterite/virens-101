# Fragment: Twin Bestiaries (Technological / Teratological)

> [!warning] Status: Seed<br/>
> Unformed idea with potential. Needs significant refinement before it can become a structural proposal or content page. Captured from G1 scene brainstorming session to preserve the conceptual spark.

## Core

A single two-column (or two-register) nexus page presenting a **Technological Bestiary** (Unix/infrastructure metaphors from the supporting pages) and a **Teratological Bestiary** (monster/creature metaphors from the narrative and theoretical apparatus) in counterpoint or juxtaposition. Both are indices of metaphors — one domesticates the alien, the other alienates the domestic. Together they perform denaturalization in opposite directions.

## The Pairing's Conceptual Work

The **Technological Bestiary** catalogs metaphors that make the unfamiliar legible through creature-language: Unix terms, RFID systems, ILS, VIRENS automation — things that behave, that have habits, that can be tamed or that bite. These are the webtext's infrastructure metaphors, the ones that make the course's digital systems approachable.

The **Teratological Bestiary** catalogs metaphors that make the familiar _strange_ again: Frankenstein, corpses, specters, vampires, golems, changelings — things that shouldn't be alive but move. These are the webtext's critical metaphors, the ones that defamiliarize rubrics, portfolios, assessment, institutional labor extraction.

One domesticates the alien; the other alienates the domestic. The complementarity _is_ denaturalization viewed from two directions.

## Frankenstein as Dual-Nature Pivot

Frankenstein is the entry that appears in both bestiaries. It is both technological (assembled from parts, animated by technique) and teratological (monster, the thing that shouldn't be alive). The rubric-as-Frankenstein insight from Bernstein sits at this intersection. In a two-column layout, Frankenstein would be the one entry that crosses the column break — the cross-reference that reveals the two lists aren't really two lists.

## Pre-Linnaean Epistemology

Both are literally bestiaries — pre-Linnaean taxonomy, classification before rationalization. The medieval bestiary doesn't distinguish real from mythical; the pelican and the basilisk sit in the same book because both are creatures that teach you something. The epistemological stance the pairing imports: these metaphors aren't decorative, they're pedagogical instruments, and the distinction between "real" technical terminology and "figurative" monster language is the kind of naturalized boundary the article wants to disturb.

## Bidirectional Hyperlink Infrastructure

Each monster metaphor hyperlinks to its bestiary entry; each bestiary entry hyperlinks back to every page where the metaphor appears. The bestiary becomes a map of the article's own figurative infrastructure — a reader can see which metaphors cluster where, which ones appear in Grant's track vs. Vera's vs. the nexus pages. This is **textual accumulation performed on the webtext itself**: the article's own metaphorical labor made visible through indexical structure.

Grant's "spectral" hyperlink in G1 would point into the teratological column, arriving from a sensory word in a fiction page and landing in a scholarly-conceptual index. The reader who follows it discovers the webtext has been tracking monstrosity as a conceptual thread. The first hyperlink in Grant's track leads somewhere unexpected — signaling that his pages work differently from the nexus pages.

## Relationship to Existing Bestiary Content

The current `content/supporting/bestiary.md` ("The Garden Tended / The Monster Zoo") contains substantial teratological material — Specter, Doppelgänger, Revenant, Mummy, Golem, Changeling, Vampire — organized by academic calendar. **This content should not remain a final content page in its current form.** It needs reverting to fragment status and rethinking as raw material for the twin-bestiary structure. The calendar-organized monster sequence may survive as the teratological column's organizing principle, but the page architecture changes fundamentally if the bestiary becomes a two-column nexus page rather than a standalone supporting page.

The technological column does not yet exist as content. Its raw material is scattered across the supporting pages' Unix-themed terminology, the init page's infrastructure metaphors, and the course documentation's tool language.

## Open Questions

- **Structural status**: Is this a nexus node, a supporting page, or something new? Nexus nodes do theoretical work; bestiaries do _indexical_ work. That distinction might be worth preserving rather than collapsing. But the conceptual argument about denaturalization-in-two-directions _is_ theoretical work.
- **Visual design**: Two columns, two sections, or two visual registers? Side-by-side makes the complementarity immediately visible. Or does separation matter — two pages that the reader discovers are mirrors only after visiting both?
- **Growth**: Does the teratological bestiary grow as the reader moves through the webtext? If entries accumulate through encounter rather than existing all at once, the bestiary performs textual accumulation as reading experience. Technical feasibility TBD.
- **Scope for Kairos submission**: Does a twin-bestiary nexus page fit within the submission scope, or is this companion-vault territory? The bidirectional hyperlink infrastructure adds production complexity.
- **Relationship to QUANTITATION sequence**: If this becomes a nexus node, does it claim one of the twelve positions or sit outside the sequence? The bestiary's indexical function is categorically different from the argumentative function of existing nexus nodes.
- **What populates the technological column?** Need an inventory of Unix/infrastructure metaphors currently deployed across the webtext. Some candidates: daemon, pipe, fork, root, shell, mount, kill, zombie (process), orphan (process) — several of which have their own teratological resonances.
