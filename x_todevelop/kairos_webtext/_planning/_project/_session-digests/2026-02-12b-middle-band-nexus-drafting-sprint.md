### 3. Argumentative spine of positions 5–9 confirmed

The five pages form a tight chain:

- **Taxus (5):** Generation before evaluation is economic necessity — premature constraint destroys irreversible experience-good value. Recovers Elbow structurally against social-turn dismissal. Connects to Quercus (Arrow's learning-by-doing) and Nerium (accumulation requires raw material).
- **Ilex (6):** Difficulty is diagnostic signal revealing infrastructural constraints invisible until breakdown. Fuses Star (infrastructure visibility upon breakdown) with Salvatori (difficulty as access to invisible processes). Generation (Taxus) produces the encounters; difficulty is the information those encounters yield.
- **Thuja (7):** Constraints have designable properties — visibility, readability, writability, ownership — analyzable via Unix permission-flags heuristic. Provides the analytical toolkit Alnus needs to sequence constraint properties across time. Distinguishes constraint sequencing from scaffolding: scaffolding is removed; constraint ownership changes hands.
- **Alnus (8):** Constraint sequencing is the article's primary theoretical contribution. Two courses with identical constraint sets produce different outcomes if constraints arrive in different sequences. Temporal architecture of arrival shapes learning more than constraint content. Alexander's dependency decomposition provides the design logic; Meadows's leverage points explain why reordering is higher-leverage than content changes.
- **Tsuga (9):** Citation as dependency management — not formatting compliance but structural load-bearing relationships within arguments. BEAM taxonomy as dependency-type distinction. Howard's patchwriting as unresolved dependency signal. Applies constraint-sequencing logic specifically to source integration.

## Formulation Candidates

1. **[Taxus]** "Premature constraint does not merely limit a writer's options — it destroys value that cannot be recovered, because the experience that would have produced it did not occur." — Core claim; the irreversibility thesis connecting generation to experience-good economics.

2. **[Taxus]** "You cannot recover the felt sense that was never permitted to emerge, the constraint that was never encountered because the generative attempt that would have revealed it was truncated by an earlier constraint, the misfit that would have taught the writer where her argument's dependencies actually lie." — The irreversibility paragraph's key sentence.

3. **[Alnus]** "Two courses with identical constraint sets — same formatting requirements, same citation expectations, same rubric criteria — will produce different learning outcomes if the constraints arrive in different sequences." — Opening provocation; the article's central claim stated maximally.

4. **[Alnus]** "A course that assigns citation formatting in week two is not being rigorous — it is resolving a dependent constraint before its foundation exists." — The practical punchline of Alexander's dependency logic applied to FYC.

5. **[Thuja]** "This is what distinguishes constraint sequencing from mere scaffolding: scaffolding is removed as competence develops, but constraints are not removed — their ownership changes hands." — Key distinction positioning the framework against the Vygotskian scaffolding metaphor.

6. **[Ilex]** "The fusion of Star and Salvatori is the move this page makes. Star gives the structural principle: infrastructure is invisible until breakdown. Salvatori gives the pedagogical method: difficulty, treated as diagnostic, is the mechanism by which invisible processes become available for reflection." — States the conceptual fusion explicitly.

7. **[Tsuga]** "A student who cites Salvatori is not just avoiding plagiarism — she is declaring a dependency: her argument requires Salvatori's framework to function, and if Salvatori's claims were to be dismantled, the student's argument would need restructuring." — The dependency claim in its concrete form.
