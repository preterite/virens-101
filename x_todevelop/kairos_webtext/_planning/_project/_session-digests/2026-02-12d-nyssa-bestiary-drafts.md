# Session Digest: Nyssa Bestiary Draft + Nyssa Page Draft

**Date:** 2026-02-12

Two major outputs written to disk: (1) "The Garden Tended / The Monster Zoo," a ~2,400-word supplementary bestiary page linked from Nyssa, mapping eight monsters to the fall semester calendar and Grant's narrative scenes; (2) a full Nyssa nexus page draft at ~1,526 words, structured around the Bernstein reversal ("the rubric is Frankenstein") and the dysraphism concept, closing the economic cycle from Quercus while structurally refusing closure. ## Theoretical Breakthroughs

### 1. "The Rubric Is Frankenstein" — The Bernstein Reversal Applied to Assessment

The session's primary contribution. Close reading of Bernstein's "Blood on the Cutting Room Floor" produced the insight that the essay's central move is a reversal: assembling a whole from autonomous parts is not what produces monsters. "What made Dr. Frankenstein's creature a monster was the reverse — starting with a preconceived whole and tailoring the parts to fit." Applied to composition pedagogy: the rubric that specifies what the assembled portfolio should look like before the assembling begins is the preconceived whole. The student tailoring fragments to fit predetermined assessment categories is performing Frankenstein's error. This compressed into the formulation "the rubric is Frankenstein" — identified as possibly the single most quotable formulation in the entire webtext.

Critical qualification developed alongside: rubrics are not inherently Frankensteinian. A rubric that assesses the quality of relationships between parts, visibility of process, evidence of selection serves as "the rhapsodist's audience, listening for how the stitching was done." The distinction is between rubrics that specify the shape of the whole in advance and rubrics that assess the quality of the assembly as enacted. This distinction is essential to prevent the formulation from being received as anti-assessment, which would make it easy to dismiss.

### 2. Dysraphism as the Positive Design Principle

Bernstein's dysraphism (from *rhaphe*, seam; dysraphism = congenital misseaming) gives Nyssa its positive alternative to the preconceived whole. Connected to Walter Ong's rhapsode — the oral performer who stitches formulaic elements into sequences without a blueprint. The chain: Ong's rhapsode → Bernstein's dysraphism → the VIRENS student assembling a portfolio from fragments generated under different constraints at different moments. The portfolio's coherence comes from articulation of parts rather than conformity to a template. Process archaeology (tracing an idea across fragments) is dysraphic reading: following the seam, not the surface.

Key insight: writing is the technology that made the preconceived-whole problem possible. You cannot impose a rubric on an oral performance because the performance doesn't exist until the performer performs it. The rubric requires fixity; fixity requires writing; writing enables Frankenstein.

### 3. The Non-Redemption Framing

The corpse-compost-garden arc, as theorized through Bernstein, is not a redemption narrative. Dead things do not come back to beautiful life. The seams stay visible. The joints don't close. The blood stays on the floor. The arc describes a process in which non-resolution is productive — the dysraphic tension between parts generates the energy for the next cycle. This reframes the arc away from the reasons document's more optimistic presentation (narrative coherence, emotional validation, process visibility) toward a flatter, more structural account.

Bernstein's final image — "the only mark of its past, of its having been made, is the blood on the cutting room floor" — does triple duty as corpse (evidence of cutting), evidence of labor, and condition of the living text.

### 4. Teratology as the Blurring of Self/Other

Michael's question: "What is teratology at its core: a fear of self-deformation or a fear of the Other?" The session's answer: neither exactly — the distinction doesn't hold. The monster is the moment the line between self-deformation and the Other dissolves. Something you made stops being yours. The monster is assembled parts whose autonomy returns. This connects to the changeling (the student who comes back from college transformed), the revenant (the fragment that returns changed), and the golem (animated by inscription, denied full membership).

### 5. Corpse/Compost/Garden as Literal Economic Cycle Stages

The three-stage arc maps onto the Marxian cycle from Quercus — not metaphorically but structurally. Corpse = production (material generated without predetermined purpose, value latent). Compost = distribution (material moves between contexts through peer review, reclassification, source integration; value transformed through circulation). Garden = use that feeds back into production (portfolio as cultivation, not consumption; selection generates new understanding that becomes the next corpse). The claim is that this mapping is not figurative: the economic cycle is literal, and the organic vocabulary names what it feels like from inside.

Flagged risk: the "not metaphorical" claim will draw pushback from readers who see calling student writing "corpses" as obviously figural. Defense available but not deployed in draft: the economic cycle is literal (real labor, real value, real institutional extraction); the organic vocabulary is figural; the structure the vocabulary describes is not.

## Architectural Decisions

### 1. Bestiary as Supplementary Page in `content/supporting/`

The monster zoo lives at `content/supporting/bestiary.md` rather than as a planning fragment or as part of the Nyssa nexus page. It is published content linked from Nyssa and leading to Contributing. This resolves the surplus-material question from session 2026-02-12c: the literary-spectral material lives in the bestiary; the economic-structural argument lives on the Nyssa page.

### 2. Eight Monsters, Seven with Calendar Hooks

The bestiary contains eight monsters: the vampire (unseasonable, no narrative scene) plus seven mapped to fall semester calendar moments. The vampire's lack of season is a deliberate structural feature — extraction is continuous, not episodic. The seven seasonal monsters map to Grant's narrative scenes:

| Monster | Calendar | Week | Stage | Core Anxiety |
|---------|----------|------|-------|--------------|
| Specter | Labor Day | 1–2 | Corpse | Persistence of what was never realized |
| Doppelgänger | Indigenous Peoples' Day | 6–7 | Compost | Irreconcilable doubles in same form |
| Revenant | Homecoming | 7–8 | Compost | The past returning embodied and changed |
| Mummy | Halloween | 8–9 | Compost | Preservation against decomposition |
| Golem | Veterans Day | 10–11 | Compost→Garden | Animation by inscription, denied membership |
| Changeling | Thanksgiving | 13–14 | Garden | Irreversible transformation, unrecognition |
| Ghost | Winter Break | 15 | Post-Garden | Unrealized configurations, dormant corpus |

### 3. Golem for Veterans Day (Not Zombie or Prosthesis)

Resolved after deliberation. The golem connects to Grant's GI-descended Filipino-American family through the inscription/animation/rescission structure (Rescission Act of 1946 as erasing the letter: *emet* → *met*). The golem is animated by paperwork, serves faithfully, is denied full membership — mapping the Filipino veteran experience. Veterans Day scene uses the circulation desk and a display as material details. The word "circulation" does double duty.

The vampire was considered for Veterans Day but reassigned to the unseasonable position. Zombie was considered but risks being reductive about veteran experience. Prosthesis/disability rhetoric was considered but deemed a different argument.

### 4. Nyssa Draft Written — Register Break Confirmed

Nyssa breaks register in its final passage, speaking directly to the reader and naming the webtext itself as dysraphic and assembled. The Contributing link is embedded in the final sentence as inline prose ("Take what is useful. Compost the rest.") rather than as a navigation element. This confirms Option B from session 2026-02-12c. The page is ~1,526 words, shorter than Nerium (~1,884) as planned — Nyssa trusts the other nexus pages to have done their work and builds only what they didn't.

### 5. Nyssa Frontmatter Corrected

The placeholder had incorrect frontmatter (concept: "Difficulty-as-Diagnostic", difficulty-pair referencing Tsuga). Draft corrects to concept: "Corpse, Compost, Garden — Decomposition Over Preservation" with appropriate connections, scholars, and primary-theorist fields.

## Discarded Directions

1. **Navigational bifurcation at Nyssa.** Considered as a structural option: the reader arriving at Nyssa encounters a choice between the economic-cycle reading and the literary-spectral reading, both converging on Contributing. Rejected in favor of the cleaner architecture: economic argument on the main page, literary-spectral material in the bestiary. The bifurcation option was the most structurally ambitious but risked making Nyssa feel like a different kind of page than the other eleven nexus pages in a way that would be confusing rather than productive.

2. **Decomposing Nyssa into its own compost.** Considered as a meta-structural option: Nyssa as the one nexus page that splits its own content outward — some to Contributing, some to a fragment, some to navigational margins. Partially enacted (the bestiary is material composted out of the nexus page) but the full decomposition was rejected because the main page still needs to carry a coherent argument, and fragmenting that argument across locations would sacrifice clarity for cleverness.

3. **Zombie for Veterans Day.** Considered and rejected as potentially reductive about veteran experience — implying veterans are "bodies without consciousness animated by violent history." The golem is more respectful and more structurally rich (inscription/animation/membership).

4. **Easter/spring seasonal possibilities.** Session corrected a full-academic-year assumption to fall-semester 15-week framework. Lazarus-as-changeling, ironic zombie (Easter), and Pentecost ghost/Babel connections were all considered for spring placement. Discarded because the course calendar is fall-only. Michael expressed regret at losing the Easter possibilities. Some of this material (especially Pentecost/Babel/inhabiting-the-Other) could migrate to a different project or to the "Rhetoric Without Rhetors" article.
