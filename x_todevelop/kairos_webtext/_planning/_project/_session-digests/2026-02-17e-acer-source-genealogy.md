## Theoretical Breakthroughs / Key Developments

### The Mediating Source Is Already Pedagogical

**Metadata:**
- Domain/Thread(s): acer-nexus, technology-definition
- Type: evidentiary
- Dashboard-worthy: yes
- Captured in: sources-acer.md

**Discovery:** The "technology substitutes capital-intensive processes for labor-intensive processes" formulation does not come directly from Marx. It comes from C. Paul Olson's "Who Computes?" (1987), a chapter about computing technology in education within a critical pedagogy anthology. Marx describes mechanization substituting constant capital for variable capital as a *tendency* of capitalist production; Olson renders the relationship *definitional* and applies it to educational technology specifically. The "-processes" suffix originates with Olson.

**Implications:**
- The argument that capital-for-labor substitution applies to educational settings was already being made within critical pedagogy in the late 1980s — Edwards is extending, not importing
- The genealogy Marx → Olson → Edwards shows increasing specificity at each link: historical tendency → definitional (educational technology) → extended to writing itself
- This genealogy is itself argumentatively productive and could appear in Acer nexus prose rather than being compressed into a parenthetical citation

**Next steps:** Retrieve Olson page number from physical copy or library request.
