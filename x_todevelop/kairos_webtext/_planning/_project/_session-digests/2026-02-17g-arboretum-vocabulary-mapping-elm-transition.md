## Theoretical Breakthroughs / Key Developments

### Girdling Assigned to Hemlock as Et In Arcadia Ego

**Metadata:**
- Domain/Thread(s): arboretum, citation-as-dependency
- Type: architectural, conceptual
- Dashboard-worthy: yes
- Captured in: this digest

**Discovery:** Girdling — the violent, visible, deliberate severing of a tree's cambium — belongs at the hemlock, not the elm. Botanical reasoning: DED works through the vascular system internally (invisible), not through bark damage. A botanically literate reader would catch the error. But girdling hemlock is real practice: land managers girdle heavily adelgid-infested hemlocks to prevent spread, a triage decision to sacrifice one tree for the stand. In an untended-but-not-abandoned arboretum, a girdled hemlock is evidence of the last management decision someone made before walking away.

**Thematic reasoning:** The girdling scar appears in the wetforest section — the most immersive, quietest, most enclosed part of the walk. The walker has been lulled by the cedar screen, the alder's pale bark, the deep duff. The violence arrives in the peaceful space, not the ominous one. The toxic garden announces its strangeness; the wet forest conceals its wound.

**Cambium call-and-response:** Cambium deployed at the yew (Group 2) describes survival — "what remains is living cambium around nothing." Cambium (or its destruction) at the hemlock (Group 3) describes severance — the girdling ring where the living layer was cut. Two fates for the same tissue, separated by the cedar-alder passage.

**Implications:**
- Girdling is used exactly once in the arboretum text — its force depends on singularity
- The hemlock passage carries both the deepest immersion and the sharpest disruption
- Resonance with nexus concept (citation as dependency): girdling severs the dependency network; the tree still stands but connections that made it alive are broken
- The arboretum reads as untended but bearing evidence of past decisions — this is the clearest such evidence

**Next steps:** Draft hemlock passage with girdling scar as the central image. Decide how much of the adelgid story to imply vs. leave silent.

### Backward Reference Convention: Enacted, Not Reported

**Metadata:**
- Domain/Thread(s): arboretum, IF-echo
- Type: architectural
- Dashboard-worthy: no
- Captured in: this digest

**Decision:** The arboretum does not use parser-style backward references ("The large oak is now to your south"). Compass orientation can appear but must be embedded in perception rather than inventory. "To the east, the lawn fades back into overgrowth" works. "The building is now behind you to the west" does not — that's a state report.

**Rationale:** Explicit compass room-change announcements would name the IF game, violating the "enacted, never named" principle established in digest 2026-02-17f. The oak stays present through contrast (diminishing openness, arriving shade) rather than directional naming.

## Architectural Decisions / Structural Changes

### DED Elm as Princeton Cultivar Replacement in Allée Remnant

**Decision:** The mismatched elm pair is an allée remnant: American elms that once formed a longer row, with one gap filled by a shorter Princeton cultivar (DED-resistant hybrid). A lacebark elm (U. parvifolia) stands off to one side as the collection's taxonomic range.

**Rationale:** The Princeton cultivar's presence tells the DED story without naming the disease — the asymmetry (one shorter, younger tree in a row of taller ones) signals replacement to anyone who knows elms. The passage names species ("a Princeton cultivar, shorter, ten years in the ground at most") without explaining why. The narrator's species-naming license is established: the voice reads trees the way the opening frame said trees are "labeled in a language you may or may not already read."

**Implications:**
- Three elms with three visual signatures: American elm vase shape, Princeton hybrid (similar but shorter), lacebark with mottled exfoliating bark
- The allée remnant is an incomplete corridor — the walker passes through architecture that announces its own incompleteness
- No sentence says "Dutch elm disease." The asymmetry says it.

### Arboretum Temporal Register: "Outside of Time"

**Decision:** The arboretum is untended and human-empty but not abandoned — "outside of time." Seasonal rules are slightly suspended. Acorns and samaras coexist on the ground (botanically, sugar maple samaras and white oak acorns overlap in autumn; red maple samaras persist as debris from spring). The opening frame's "the grass is cut but not recently" sets up this temporal looseness.

**Rationale:** A ground surface with acorns and old samaras reads as a place where things fall and stay. This is more interesting than strict seasonal accuracy and serves the arboretum's character as a curated space that has drifted from active management. The girdled hemlock is the strongest evidence of past human presence; the mown-but-not-recently lawn is the subtlest.

### Maple Group: Sugars and Reds for Contrast

**Decision:** Multiple maples — taller sugar maples and smaller planted red maples — in a small group. Samaras scattered on the ground. The maple group is the last familiar space before the walk gets strange; spatially, it's where the walker is *among* trees for the first time (oak was solitary approach, elms were corridor, maples are cluster/enclosure).

**Rationale:** Michael's northeastern experience: maples in small groups, reds for contrast next to taller sugars. The samara detail anchors the passage in bodily memory (everyone has seen helicoptering seeds). The progressive enclosure — solitary → corridor → cluster — sets up the transition into Group 2.

## Open Questions

- **How much of the adelgid story to include at the hemlock?** The girdling scar is decided; the question is whether the text names the woolly adelgid or lets the wound stand unexplained. Naming it would parallel the elm's unnamed DED — but girdling is a human act, not a disease symptom, so the text may need to gesture at *why* someone did this. — Urgency: medium; Blocking: no; For: hemlock passage drafting

- **Oleander: near-zero vocabulary density as deliberate silence?** The vocabulary map assigns almost no specialized terms to oleander, on the theory that the absence of technical language mirrors the plant's trick — it doesn't present as something that needs caution. Is this too clever? Or does the oleander passage need at least one term to maintain the register contract? — Urgency: low; Blocking: no; For: oleander passage drafting

- **Stone wall reservation:** Stone wall reserved from oak-to-elm transition (Adventure closing already has "brick building"; wall may be needed later). Where, if anywhere, does a low stone wall appear in the walk? Candidates: transition into Group 2, or closing frame only. — Urgency: low; Blocking: no; For: later spatial planning

- **Lacebark elm placement:** Lacebark off to one side of the allée, or integrated into the row? Off to the side reads as a specimen planting (taxonomic range); in the row reads as another replacement. — Urgency: low; Blocking: no; For: elm passage drafting

- **"Absence of field" phrase:** Michael has kept the phrase through multiple revisions ("a path's absence of field"), and it's conceptually strong (defining the path negatively). The possessive grammar creates backward-parsing. Current placement in revised opening may still need loosening. — Urgency: low; Blocking: no; For: opening frame polish

## Discarded Directions

- **Parser-style backward references ("The large oak is now to your south"):** Rejected as too explicitly Zork — reads as room-change state report, names the IF game. The oak stays present through contrast (diminishing openness, arriving shade) rather than compass inventory.

- **Girdling at the elm:** Rejected on botanical grounds — DED works internally through the vascular system, not by bark damage. A girdled elm would be a false note in the naturalist register. The elm's violence is absence and replacement, not visible wounding.

- **Literal walled garden for Group 2:** Reserved rather than rejected — a built wall isn't needed if the land itself (south-facing shelf, narrowing path, dense planting) creates enclosure. But "walled garden" as concept still available if spatial logic demands it.

## Vocabulary Density Map

Reference for drafting — where each specialized term fits tonally and botanically:

| Section | Key Terms | Density | Notes |
|---------|-----------|---------|-------|
| Oak | bole, crown | light | establishing contract with reader |
| Elm | cultivar, exfoliating, allée | light | familiar territory, species names carry weight |
| Maple | samara | one strong term | contextually clear from bodily memory |
| *Transition 1* | ecotone, aspect | two terms marking shift | the vocabulary itself signals new territory |
| Oleander | (habit) or near-zero | minimal | silence mirrors the plant's trick |
| Yew | heartwood, cambium, aril | denser | language gets strange with the space |
| Holly | understory, drupe, (heterophylly) | moderate | looking down for first time |
| *Transition 2* | (canopy closure) | one term | felt overhead |
| Cedar | spray/frond, fibrous, (volatile) | moderate | environmental vocabulary begins |
| Alder | strobiles, clade, pioneer, lenticels | densest passage | the vocabulary peak |
| Hemlock | duff, girdling, (climax) | moderate | environmental + the wound |
| *Transition 3* | swale | one term | felt underfoot |
| Star Anise | follicle, volatile | moderate | volatile echoes cedar |
| Olive | coppice, rootstock, graft union | moderate-dense | all about joining/grafting |
| Tupelo | senescence, bole (3rd use), buttress | culminating | terms carry accumulated force |

Density arc: sparse → thickening → peak at alder → slight thinning → culminating weight at tupelo.

Three-use thread: **bole** appears at oak (massive), yew (hollow), tupelo (alligator-hide) — vertical presence across the walk.

Cambium call-and-response: **cambium** at yew (survival) → **girdling** at hemlock (severance).

General principle: deploy ~1 specialized term per passage, sometimes 2-3, with some passages deliberately bare. Total across 12 trees + transitions: ~20-25 terms. Enough to flavor the register without becoming a glossary.

Additional terms to deploy opportunistically: cambium (yew or hemlock), mast (oak, if "acorns" feels too plain), phenology (maple or opening frame, re: temporal looseness), canopy (multiple uses, precise), leader (hemlock's drooping topmost shoot), snag (standing dead tree — possible at hemlock if girdled specimen is dead).
