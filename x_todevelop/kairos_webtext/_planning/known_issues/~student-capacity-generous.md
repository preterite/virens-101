# Fragment: Students Underestimate Intelligence

## Core

The students composition typically serves are smarter than they know, and a core pedagogical task is helping them discover capacities they don't yet recognize.

## Elaboration

Link to Bartholomae and Petrosky in Facts, Artifacts, Counterfacts, and their intro to Ways of Reading: ask students to do hard things and they'll surprise. This is the rationale for Bartholomae's basic writing course. Because they're smart, they'll survive encounters with difficulty.

The article and apparatus with Elbow's implication toward a generous understanding of students as [[600_teaching/virens_101/x_todevelop/kairos_webtext/_planning/known_issues/frag-06-embodied-agnostic|frag-06-embodied-agnostic]] embodied agnostic subjects requiring generosity. If we are to be generous toward students, then we assume like Bartholomae and Petrosky and [[@adler-kassner2015namin]] Adler-Kassner and writing about writing authors that students are capable of reading advanced texts (Salvatori's early work on difficulty) and capable of intelligent analysis -- so we assume then that diminished capacity comes from _attention_ as it often does, and especially for students, and attention is a time-based phenomenon.

This assumption shapes the course's orientation toward student agency and the "interest = attention + time" formulation. Students don't know what interests them because interest requires compound investment they haven't yet made. The assumption also connects to the agnostic subject: we can't know their interiorities, but we can create conditions where they surprise themselves through accumulated textual work. The course is designed to help students discover capacities, not to fix deficiencies.
