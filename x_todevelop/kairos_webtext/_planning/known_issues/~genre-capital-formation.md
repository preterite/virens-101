# Fragment: Past as Store — Genre Theory and the Economy Frame

## Core

Genre theory treats the writer's past as _formation_ — how institutions and discourse shaped current repertoires. Constraint structure treats the writer's past as _capital_ — accumulated resources available for recirculation and compounding. Both accounts are true, but they ask different questions and produce different pedagogies.

## Elaboration

This is the sharpest distinction from genre theory and the strongest answer to the "just different terms for the same thing" critique. The two frames share a premise — no writer starts from zero; everyone arrives historically constituted — but diverge in what they foreground:

**Genre theory asks**: What recurring situation is this? What social action does this genre perform? What uptakes are available? The gaze is _outward_ toward social patterns. The writer's past matters as the history of discourse communities encountered, conventions internalized, rhetorical situations recognized. Bawarshi, Devitt, and the uptake literature describe this dynamically — genre knowledge isn't static but evolves through repeated encounters. The writer is _formed by_ genres.

**Constraint structure asks**: What have I already made that I can build on? What textual resources do I own? Where can I reinvest? The gaze is _backward_ toward one's own textual history. The writer's past matters as stored labor available for reconfiguration and reproduction. Writing lets what you did _stay with you_, available for compounding. The writer _accumulates_ through writing.

The pedagogical consequence: genre-based courses help students read situations (what does this context call for?). Accumulation-based courses help students see resources (what do I already have to work with?). For first-year writers — who often feel they have nothing to say, nothing to draw on, nothing to offer — the second question may be more immediately enabling than the first. Helping students see what they _have_ can precede helping them see what shaped them.

This is _both/and_, not either/or. Students are shaped by genre patterns (true) and accumulate textual capital (also true). The article's claim isn't that genre theory is wrong but that the economy frame adds something genre theory's vocabulary doesn't capture: the mechanism by which past writing becomes future resource, the compounding that makes an hour in Week 12 more productive than an hour in Week 2, the way textual capital grows through circulation rather than sitting inert.

The key terminological distinction: "capital" is not Bourdieu's metaphorical capital (cultural capital, symbolic capital — analogies between domains). It's Gibson-Graham's literal economic vocabulary applied to non-market production. When a student revises a fragment from Week 3 into a portfolio section in Week 13, that's not _like_ economic activity — it _is_ economic activity: labor applied to existing capital, producing new value through transformation.

## Open

- How to engage genre theory's uptake/repertoire literature without strawmanning? (Devitt's _Writing Genres_ is the fairest target — she's the most dynamic genre theorist)
- Is "capital" still the right term given connotation risks, or does the Gibson-Graham grounding resolve this?
- Connection to Trimbur on circulation — circulation is what makes stored material into active capital
- Connection to transfer research (Yancey, Nowacek, Beaufort) — transfer is partly what this frame redescribes in economic terms
- Does the both/and framing risk seeming like the economy frame is merely supplemental? Need to show what it _reveals_ that genre theory alone can't see
