Design Analysis

The dollar sign (A, G) is the most semantically precise but least visually interesting. For non-technical readers, it's either noise or an unintended economic pun. It also doesn't scale down well into nav context — at 0.9rem it becomes punctuation clutter.

The solid block (B, C) is the strongest brutalist option. Chunky, geometric, high-contrast, holds weight against Fraunces 700. Immediately reads as "cursor" to terminal users and as "bold decorative mark" to everyone else. The blinking variant (C) adds semantic richness ("waiting for input") but technically violates the design spec's "static decorative elements only" principle.

The bitmap cursor (D, E) is the most theoretically resonant option. The visible pixel grid performs the denaturalization argument: even the cursor — the most basic interface element — is shown to be constructed from discrete, countable units. The 50% fill creates a halftone/screen effect that connects to print production history. At heading scale, the individual pixels should be visible; at nav scale, it may collapse to a lighter-toned block (which is fine — it degrades gracefully).

The outline block (H) is interesting as meta-commentary (you're outside the system, looking in) but may be too subtle — the 2.5px border is thin relative to the design vocabulary's 4-8px borders.

Recommendation: E (bitmap, blinking) as primary, falling back to D (bitmap, static) via prefers-reduced-motion. The pixel grain does genuine theoretical work. The blink is arguably functional (signals command-line context) rather than decorative, which may justify the exception to the static-only principle. If the blink feels like too much, B (solid static block) is the clean fallback that still reads as cursor without the bitmap complexity.
