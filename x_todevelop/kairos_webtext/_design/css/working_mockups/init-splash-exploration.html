Design Notes

A (Light + Tight) is the safest. The misregistration is a texture that rewards attention — you notice the color fringes when you look — but the page reads cleanly at first glance. The light background matches the nexus/supporting page palette, so it feels like it belongs to the same family as env and the other supporting pages. The risk is it's too tame: the broadside energy gets domesticated by the clean layout.

B (Dark + Wide) is the screamiest. The dark field makes the color ghosts visible as actual chromatic events, not just texture. The wider offsets mean you can't miss the misregistration — it's the first thing you see. On a dark background, the four track colors glow. This is the most "Kairos hasn't seen this" option. The risk is that the dark field creates a strong visual break from the rest of the webtext (all three tracks and supporting pages use light backgrounds), which could feel jarring. But: init is the one page that should feel different — it's the space before the tracks exist.

C (Split Register) does the most theoretical work. Giving CONSTRAINT warm ghosts (student + vault) and STRUCTURE cool ghosts (designer + nexus) maps the two words to the webtext's two modes: constraint is what students experience and what the apparatus documents; structure is what the designer builds and what the nexus theorizes. The four-color rule bar makes the mapping explicit without explaining it. The risk is that this mapping is too clever by half — it might not be legible to any reader who hasn't already read the webtext, which is all of them at this point.

All three use the 5×7 bitmap cursor on the env link only, establishing it as the primary action. The link hierarchy is consistent: env (5px border, track nexus green) → manifest + index (3px, plum) → tertiary (2px, gray). The tertiary links are available but clearly subordinate.

Open question: The tertiary links (spec, references, contributing, changelog) could be dropped from init entirely. A reader arriving for the first time doesn't need them; they're accessible from the right-nav on every subsequent page. Removing them would make init even sparser — title, subtitle, author, env, manifest, index, done.
